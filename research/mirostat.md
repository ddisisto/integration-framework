# Mirostat and the Three-Regime Observation: Prior Art, Citation Trail, and Gap Analysis

Research compiled 2026-03-22. Sources: arXiv, Semantic Scholar, OpenReview, web search. Flagged where claims could not be independently verified.

---

## 1. What Mirostat Actually Claims

**Paper:** Basu, S., Ramachandran, G. S., Keskar, N. S., & Varshney, L. R. (2021). "Mirostat: A Neural Text Decoding Algorithm that Directly Controls Perplexity." ICLR 2021.

### 1.1 The Three-Regime Observation

Mirostat's foundational observation is that text generated by language models falls into three qualitatively distinct regimes depending on sampling parameters:

- **Boredom trap** (low k/p): Perplexity drops with text length. The model settles into high-probability loops, producing excessive repetition. Cross-entropy falls monotonically.
- **Confusion trap** (high k/p): Perplexity increases with text length. The model samples from the unreliable tail of the distribution, producing incoherent text. Cross-entropy rises monotonically.
- **Optimal zone** (intermediate): Perplexity remains approximately constant. The model produces coherent, non-repetitive text. Cross-entropy is stable.

The paper establishes an empirical relationship: "percentage repetition decreases with increase in cross-entropy and more importantly, for a fixed GPT-2 model, this relation is independent of the sampling method." This holds across top-k, top-p, and temperature sampling. Sentence-level repetitions vanish when cross-entropy exceeds ~2.5 bits.

### 1.2 Theoretical Basis

The theoretical framework rests on Zipf's law. Assuming token frequencies follow a Zipfian distribution p(i; s, N) = 1/(i^s * H_{N,s}), Basu et al. derive:

- **Theorem 1** (Top-k surprise): S(k) = s * log(k) + log(H_{N,s}). Surprise at rank k is logarithmic — sensitive for small k, saturating for large k.
- **Theorem 2** (Top-k cross-entropy): Nonlinear growth, saturating beyond k ~ 2000.
- **Theorems 3-4** (Top-p): Cross-entropy grows nearly linearly with p. This linearity contrasts sharply with top-k's nonlinearity and is key to why top-p behaves differently in practice.

The connection to compression is explicit but narrow: cross-entropy directly equals the minimum lossless compression rate achievable via arithmetic coding. At target surprise tau = 1.0, compression ratio is 97.66%; at tau = 3.0, 91.84%; at tau = 6.0, 85.55%. Mirostat thus "generates text with predetermined minimum lossless compression rates."

### 1.3 The Control Algorithm

**Algorithm 1 (Mirostat):**
1. Estimate Zipf exponent s-hat from previous tokens
2. Compute adaptive k from s-hat and current mu (maximum surprise)
3. Perform top-k sampling with computed k
4. Compute error: e = S(X) - tau (observed surprise minus target)
5. Update: mu = mu - eta * e (learning rate eta adjusts maximum surprise)

Initial conditions: target surprise tau, maximum cross-entropy mu = 2*tau, window size m = 100.

**Algorithm 2 (Mirostat v2):** A simplified version that skips the Zipf estimation step and directly adjusts mu based on the surprise error signal. Both algorithms produce comparable results; Mirostat v2 is what llama.cpp and most practical implementations use.

### 1.4 Human Evaluation

300-token continuations rated by 43 participants on fluency, coherence, and quality:
- Optimal performance at target surprise tau = 3.0 across all metrics
- At tau = 3.0, >50% of raters classified AI text as human-written
- Quality degrades symmetrically below (repetition) and above (incoherence) the optimal range

### 1.5 Key Authorship Note

Lav Varshney (senior author) has a broader programme connecting information theory to creative generation. He has published on rate-distortion tradeoffs in creativity, defining novelty via Bayesian surprise and studying quality-novelty tradeoffs with functional forms resembling capacity-cost relationships. Mirostat can be read as an engineering instantiation of this broader theoretical programme, though the paper itself does not make this connection explicit.

## 2. Citation Trail and Reception

### 2.1 Practical Adoption

Mirostat has been widely adopted as a *sampling method*:
- Integrated into llama.cpp (both v1 and v2) with parameters --mirostat, --mirostat-lr, --mirostat-ent
- Available in text-generation-webui, LangChain, and most local LLM inference frameworks
- Used as an alternative to top-k/top-p in practice, with tau ~ 2-3 and eta ~ 0.1 as typical values

This adoption is almost entirely *practical* — Mirostat is treated as a sampling knob, not as a theoretical contribution.

### 2.2 Theoretical Development

The three-regime observation has received remarkably little theoretical development in the citation trail. Key citing works:

**Meister et al. (2023) — Locally Typical Sampling.** The most significant theoretical successor. Meister et al. independently arrive at a similar insight from a different direction: natural language maintains relatively constant per-word information rates near the conditional entropy, and sampling should enforce this. Their typical set formulation is explicitly information-theoretic, grounded in Shannon's asymptotic equipartition property. They compare directly to Mirostat: "locally typical sampling does not require a specific target information content to be defined. Rather... it defines this quantity as the conditional entropy, choosing it dynamically." This is a genuine theoretical advance over Mirostat's fixed-target approach but does not develop the three-regime observation into a broader theoretical framework.

**Hewitt, Manning, & Liang (2022) — Truncation Sampling as Language Model Desmoothing (eta-sampling).** Frames truncation as removing smoothing artifacts from the learned distribution. Proposes eta-sampling, a hybrid of locally typical and epsilon sampling. Cites Mirostat but treats it as one of several truncation strategies, not as identifying a fundamental phenomenon.

**Finlayson et al. (2024) — Closing the Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration.** Provides a theoretical explanation for why truncation sampling works, grounding it in the softmax bottleneck. Introduces Basis-Aware Threshold (BAT) sampling. Does not engage with Mirostat's three-regime observation as a theoretical contribution.

### 2.3 What Has NOT Happened

Based on extensive search, no published work has:
- Connected Mirostat's three regimes to rate-distortion theory
- Connected the regimes to information bottleneck or compression hierarchy frameworks
- Framed the boredom/confusion traps in terms of channel capacity or sequential coding
- Proposed a continuous metric (analogous to enrichment fraction) rather than treating the regimes as discrete zones
- Connected the regime taxonomy to grounding, alignment, or ecological dynamics
- Developed the observation into a theory of *what generation is* rather than a tool for *controlling generation*

The gap is striking. The three-regime observation is one of the clearest empirical regularities in LLM generation, confirmed independently by multiple research groups (see Section 3), and yet it has been treated almost exclusively as a practical problem to be solved by better sampling, not as a phenomenon to be explained by deeper theory.

## 3. Independent Rediscoveries and the Convergent Literature

The three-regime observation has been independently discovered (or at least independently characterised) by at least five separate research programmes, using different methodologies and vocabularies. None of them cite each other comprehensively, and several do not cite Mirostat.

### 3.1 Holtzman et al. (2020) — The Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration

**Chronology:** Pre-dates Mirostat (submitted 2019, published ICLR 2020). Mirostat cites Holtzman.

**What they found:** Maximisation-based decoding (beam search) produces "degenerate" text — bland, incoherent, or stuck in repetitive loops. Pure sampling produces incoherent text because the "unreliable tail" of the distribution contains tens of thousands of low-probability tokens that collectively dominate. Nucleus sampling truncates this tail.

**Three regimes (implicit):** The paper identifies (1) the repetition/blandness failure of greedy/beam search, (2) the incoherence failure of untruncated sampling, and (3) the productive zone of nucleus sampling. These map directly to Mirostat's boredom trap, confusion trap, and optimal zone, though Holtzman et al. do not frame them as a unified taxonomy.

**Key insight for our purposes:** "The probability of a repeated phrase increases with each repetition, creating a positive feedback loop." This is the mechanism behind the boredom trap, stated precisely. Also: "Natural language rarely remains in a high probability zone for multiple consecutive time steps, instead veering into lower-probability but more informative tokens." This is close to the enrichment fraction idea — human text maintains a non-trivial rate of information-bearing tokens.

**Theoretical development:** None beyond the engineering solution (nucleus sampling). The observation is treated as a problem to solve, not a phenomenon to explain.

### 3.2 Nakaishi (2024) — Critical Phase Transition in Large Language Models

**Key finding:** A phase transition at T_c ~ 1 in GPT-2's sampling temperature. Below T_c: repetitive, ordered text with power-law correlations. At T_c: comprehensible text with critical statistical properties. Above T_c: incoherent, disordered text.

**Three regimes (explicit):** Low-T (ordered/repetitive), critical (coherent), high-T (disordered/incoherent). Maps precisely to boredom trap / optimal / confusion trap.

**Theoretical framework:** Statistical mechanics, not information theory. The integrated correlation tau diverges at the transition point. Natural language corpora (OpenWebText, WikiText) display identical critical properties at T ~ 1, suggesting natural language "inherently operates at criticality."

**Does not cite Mirostat.** Does not cite Holtzman. Frames the observation as a statistical physics phenomenon.

**Connection to DiSisto:** The criticality framing is complementary. Nakaishi locates the productive regime at a phase transition; DiSisto's framework would predict this — the productive zone is where the system maintains non-trivial statistical complexity, which is characteristic of critical systems. But Nakaishi does not develop the information-theoretic implications.

### 3.3 Mikhaylovskiy (2025) — States of LLM-generated Texts and Phase Transitions

**Key finding:** Three phases of LLM-generated text using autocorrelation analysis of GloVe embeddings:
- **Periodic (Solid)** at T < 0.7: degenerate, repetitive text with oscillatory autocorrelations
- **Critical** at T ~ 0.7–1.0: power-law autocorrelation decay
- **Amorphous (Gas)** at T > 1.0: random text with exponential autocorrelation decay

**Methodology:** The GAPELMAPER metric (GloVe Autocorrelations Power/Exponential Law Mean Absolute Percentage Error Ratio) distinguishes power-law from exponential decay in autocorrelation functions.

**Key result for DiSisto's framework:** "LLM-generated texts have exponential autocorrelations decay and thus no inherent hierarchical structure" at long ranges (6000+ words). Power-law correlations exist only in shorter chunks (<2000 words) at critical temperatures. This directly supports the prediction that enrichment fraction decays over extended generation — even at the critical temperature, long-range structure degrades.

**Does not cite Mirostat.** Uses statistical physics vocabulary (solid/critical/gas). Phase transition at T ~ 0.7–1.0, slightly lower than Nakaishi's T_c ~ 1, likely model-dependent (Mikhaylovskiy uses Qwen2.5 and Phi-3 rather than GPT-2).

### 3.4 Alabdulmohsin & Steiner (2025) — A Tale of Two Structures

**Key finding:** Natural language exhibits fractal structure in its information-theoretic complexity (bits per token — BPT sequences), with self-similarity across scales and long-range dependence. The Hurst exponent H ~ 0.70 characterises this LRD. LLM-generated text has highly variable fractal parameters, suggesting fractal analysis can detect AI-generated text.

**Three regimes (implicit):** The fractal parameters of natural language occupy a narrow range; LLM text ranges widely depending on temperature and prompting. This maps to the regime taxonomy — low temperature pushes H toward values characteristic of periodic processes, high temperature toward random processes, and the natural-language-like range is in between.

**Key contribution for DiSisto:** The strong correlation between Hurst exponent and text quality provides independent support for the idea that the productive regime is characterised by a specific statistical signature (long-range dependence with a characteristic exponent), not merely the absence of pathology. The GAGLE dataset (240,000+ articles across LLMs, temperatures, and prompting methods) is a potential validation resource.

**Publication:** ICML 2025.

### 3.5 Detection-Oriented Work

Two recent papers approach the three regimes indirectly, through the lens of AI text detection:

**DivEye (Basani & Chen, 2025):** Uses surprisal-based features for AI text detection. Key innovation: second-order surprisal (delta-squared S_t) tracks "fluctuations in the rate of surprisal change." Human text exhibits richer variability in these features. The first-order difference captures "stylistic volatility such as abrupt shifts in topic or tone." This is essentially measuring a richer version of what the enrichment fraction captures — not just the proportion of high-surprisal tokens, but the *dynamics* of surprisal transitions.

**SurpMark (2025):** Quantises token surprisal into k interpretable states (k = 6-7 optimal) and builds state-transition matrices. Compares test text to human and machine reference transitions via Jensen-Shannon divergence. Key finding: "as stronger models exhibit a vanishingly small gap in marginal surprisal distributions compared to humans, marginal statistics become ineffective, however, the 'transition gap' remains significant." This means the *dynamics* of surprisal — not its level — carry the distinguishing signal. Directly supports the idea that generation regime is characterised by surprisal trajectory structure, not just mean surprisal.

Neither DivEye nor SurpMark cites Mirostat or frames their work in terms of the three regimes. Both treat surprisal dynamics as a *detection signal* rather than evidence about what generation *is*.

## 4. The Gap: What Has Not Been Done

### 4.1 No Rate-Distortion Connection

Mirostat notes that cross-entropy equals minimum lossless compression rate. Full stop. No paper in the citation trail connects the three regimes to rate-distortion theory proper — to the tradeoff between compression rate and reconstruction fidelity, to the rate-distortion function, or to the question of what distortion measure is being optimised.

DiSisto's framework fills this gap directly: the regimes correspond to different positions on the rate-distortion curve, and the enrichment fraction is a diagnostic of *where* on that curve generation is operating.

### 4.2 No Channel Capacity / Sequential Coding Framework

Mirostat treats generation as a sampling problem — pick the right k at each step. No work in the citation trail frames generation as sequential coding through a bandwidth-limited channel. The projection bottleneck argument (internal representation vastly exceeds single-token information capacity, so generation is a sequential coding scheme) is absent from the Mirostat literature.

This is DiSisto's distinctive theoretical contribution: the autoregressive loop *is* the sequential coding scheme, and the three regimes correspond to different relationships between the message being constructed and the channel capacity available per token.

### 4.3 No Continuous Metric

Mirostat defines three zones (boredom, confusion, optimal) as discrete categories. The enrichment fraction is continuous — it varies from 0 (degenerate) to 1 (noise), with the productive range in between. No paper in the citation trail proposes an equivalent continuous measure.

Meister et al.'s locally typical sampling comes closest — they define typicality relative to conditional entropy at each position. But this is used as a *filter criterion*, not as a diagnostic metric for characterising generation quality over windows.

### 4.4 No Connection to Grounding or Alignment

No work in the citation trail connects the three regimes to the grounding debate, to alignment as a problem, or to ecological dynamics of LLM deployment. Mirostat is about generation quality; DiSisto's framework connects the same observation to distortion measure mismatch (what the model optimises vs what the receiver values), which is the formal structure of alignment.

### 4.5 No Compression Hierarchy

Mirostat treats the observation at a single scale — token-level surprisal. No work in the citation trail connects it to hierarchical compression across model layers, to the relationship between training and the distortion measure, or to the deeper question of what the weight geometry represents. DiSisto's framework embeds the three-regime observation within a multi-scale compression hierarchy (Section 1: compression continuum, Section 2: structure across depth, Section 3: autoregressive loop), making the regimes a consequence of the compression framework rather than an isolated empirical regularity.

## 5. Honest Assessment: What the Reviewer Is Right About

The reviewer who flagged Mirostat as "functionally isomorphic" to DiSisto's regime taxonomy is substantially correct at the observational level. The correspondence is:

| Mirostat | DiSisto |
|----------|---------|
| Boredom trap | Enrichment fraction ~ 0 (degenerate) |
| Confusion trap | Enrichment fraction ~ 1 (noise) |
| Optimal zone | Productive interior |
| Cross-entropy as diagnostic | Surprisal as diagnostic |
| Perplexity control via feedback | (no control algorithm proposed) |

The three-regime observation is not novel to DiSisto. It was first described by Holtzman et al. (2020), formalised by Basu et al. (2021), and independently rediscovered via statistical physics by Nakaishi (2024) and Mikhaylovskiy (2025).

What *is* novel:
1. Embedding the observation in a rate-distortion / compression hierarchy framework
2. The enrichment fraction as continuous metric (vs discrete zones)
3. The connection to channel capacity and sequential coding
4. The connection to distortion measure mismatch as the structure of alignment
5. The connection to grounding as a rate-distortion question
6. The multi-scale embedding (the regimes as a consequence of compression theory, not an isolated observation)

The paper needs to cite Mirostat and acknowledge priority on the three-regime observation. The claim should be reframed: DiSisto does not discover the three regimes, but provides the theoretical framework that *explains* them and connects them to a broader account of what LLMs are doing.

## 6. Specific Implications for the Paper

### 6.1 Claim 2 (Temperature-Enrichment Curve Is Non-Monotonic)

This is the most directly anticipated claim. Mirostat (2021) already shows that cross-entropy relates non-monotonically to generation quality. Nakaishi (2024) identifies a specific phase transition temperature. Mikhaylovskiy (2025) maps the phase diagram quantitatively.

**What to do:** Claim 2 should cite Mirostat, Nakaishi, and Mikhaylovskiy as establishing the non-monotonic relationship empirically. The novel contribution is (a) the enrichment fraction as the specific continuous metric, (b) the prediction of a distinguishable *peak* rather than just a monotonic-then-plateau pattern, and (c) the embedding within the compression hierarchy framework. The claim should be reframed from "we predict this" to "this is known; we provide the theoretical account and a specific measurable quantity."

### 6.2 Claim 1 (Enrichment Fraction Decays in Unconstrained Generation)

Less directly anticipated but partially supported. Mirostat's boredom trap is specifically about perplexity *declining* with text length at low k/p — which is enrichment decay. Mikhaylovskiy's finding that power-law correlations exist only in short chunks supports the decay prediction. But no prior work frames this as a general prediction about unconstrained generation at *any* temperature, or connects it to the compositional exhaustion mechanism DiSisto proposes.

**What to do:** Cite Mirostat's boredom trap observation as partial precedent. The novel contribution is the generality claim (decay occurs across the productive range, not just at low temperatures) and the mechanism (compositional exhaustion of the weight geometry, not just a sampling artefact).

### 6.3 Section 3 Generally

The autoregressive loop section (Section 3) should add a "Prior Observations" subsection or paragraph near the introduction of the enrichment fraction concept (~line 48-70 of 03_autoregressive_loop.qmd). This should:

1. Cite Holtzman et al. (2020) as first identifying the repetition/incoherence failure modes
2. Cite Basu et al. (2021) as formalising the three-regime taxonomy (boredom/confusion/optimal) and demonstrating feedback control
3. Cite Meister et al. (2023) as providing the information-theoretic framing via typicality
4. Cite Nakaishi (2024) and Mikhaylovskiy (2025) as identifying the regimes as phase transitions
5. State clearly: "The three-regime structure is well-established. The contribution here is the enrichment fraction as continuous metric and its embedding within the compression hierarchy."

### 6.4 Claim 3 (Bandwidth) and the Alignment Connection

Not anticipated by Mirostat or any of the related work. The connection between generation regimes and alignment-as-distortion-measure-mismatch is genuinely novel. No prior work frames the boredom/confusion traps as consequences of a bandwidth constraint on the feedback channel, or connects them to the grounding problem.

## Summary

The reviewer's comparison is apt at the observational level: Mirostat identified the same three regimes in 2021 that DiSisto's framework describes. The paper must cite Mirostat and acknowledge this.

The theoretical gap is real and large. Mirostat (and the entire subsequent literature on sampling strategies, phase transitions, and AI detection) treats the three regimes as either (a) a practical problem to solve with better sampling, or (b) a statistical physics curiosity. Nobody has embedded them in a rate-distortion / compression hierarchy framework, proposed a continuous metric, connected them to channel capacity, or linked them to alignment and grounding. That theoretical development is DiSisto's genuine contribution and should be presented as such — building on established observations, not claiming to discover them.
