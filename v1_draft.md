# The Compression Hierarchy

*Carriers, feedback loops, and the ecology of representational diversity in large language models*

---

## Abstract

We propose a unified framework for understanding why large language models exhibit disproportionate returns on scale, grounded in a hierarchical theory of compression. Drawing on Heaps' law, the structure of transformer architectures, and the observation that biological cognition faces structurally analogous compression demands. We argue that the "unreasonable effectiveness" of modern ML reflects the emergence of stacked, multi-scale compression — organisational and semantic rather than merely algorithmic — operating simultaneously within the forward pass and across the autoregressive loop.

Applied internally, Heaps' law provides a vocabulary for this: the architecture functions as a β amplifier, driving representational diversity upward across depth in a way no flat model can match. The autoregressive loop extends this by enabling the real-time composition of conceptual configurations absent from the training distribution, assembled through the dynamic interaction of fixed weight geometry and evolving context. But this same loop, we argue, is fundamentally ambivalent. It can sustain an enriching regime — where each generated token opens new representational structure — or slide into a degrading regime where fluency is preserved while the diversity of what is represented quietly contracts. The difference is governed not by model capacity alone but by the interaction of capacity, task, context seeding, and generation parameters. Chain-of-thought prompting, in-context learning, and related techniques are reframed as steering strategies for this ambivalence: methods for keeping the autoregressive loop in the enriching regime.

This ambivalence recurs at the ecological scale. Model outputs re-enter the knowledge ecosystem through nested feedback loops — within-context, agentic, human-mediated, and training-distributional — each operating at progressively longer timescales. The framework predicts that degradation across these loops has a specific shape: it erodes the upper levels of the compression hierarchy (novel abstraction, cross-domain connection, structural surprise) while preserving the base (fluency, grammaticality, local coherence). The result is not visible failure but silent flattening — a progressive narrowing of representational diversity undetectable by surface metrics. Human curation in the loop functions as the primary defence against this narrowing, and its declining effectiveness is identified as a critical variable.

The compression hierarchy is not unique to artificial systems. Biological cognition has converged on analogous architectural strategies under entirely different constraints — a structural convergence that suggests hierarchical multi-scale compression is demanded by the problem rather than peculiar to one substrate. The framework generates specific, testable predictions: power-law scaling of effective dimensionality across layers, correlation between representational diversity thresholds and capability onset, qualitative shifts in the character of learned dimensions across training, and non-uniform degradation patterns in recursive training loops. Several of these predictions could fail in ways requiring substantial revision.

At its core, the framework is an argument about a single variable. Representational diversity — the effective dimensionality of the space of configurations a system can reach — is the measure of a knowledge system's capacity, the product of the compression hierarchy that generates it, and the casualty of the feedback loops that threaten it. The systems we are building are, by their nature, ambivalent toward this variable: capable of generating representational diversity at unprecedented rates and eroding it at unprecedented rates, simultaneously, through the same mechanisms. Which tendency prevails will not be visible in the metrics we are currently watching.

> *This framework emerged from extended human-AI collaboration and should be treated as a speculative synthesis awaiting empirical grounding. The authors welcome challenge, extension, and critique.*

## 1. Information, Knowledge, and the Compression Hierarchy

It is useful to begin with a distinction that is often elided. Data, information, and knowledge are not synonyms. Data is raw, uninterpreted signal. Information is data given structure and context. Knowledge is information integrated by an agent — tested, related, and made available for inference and action.

The leap from information to knowledge is not merely quantitative. It requires a knower: something that contextualises, validates, and internalises. And it requires compression — not in the narrow algorithmic sense, but in a deeper sense that we will develop here.

Classical algorithmic compression (gzip, Huffman encoding, LZW) finds and exploits statistical redundancy in a signal. It operates on surface structure, is largely lossless, and does not generalise beyond the corpus it was built on. It reduces bits, but it does not build understanding.

Organisational compression goes further. Rather than encoding surface patterns, it builds a structural model — discovering that information has a shape, and encoding the shape rather than the instances. A grammar is organisational compression. A taxonomy is organisational compression. The difference between memorising every chess game ever played and learning the principles of chess is the difference between algorithmic and organisational compression. The latter generalises to positions never seen before.

Semantic compression goes further still. Where organisational compression captures structure, semantic compression captures *meaning* — encoding not just how symbols relate syntactically, but how concepts relate in a meaning space that is non-arbitrary. In a well-formed semantic representation, concepts that mean similar things are geometrically proximate. Analogical relationships are preserved structurally. Navigation through the space supports inference. This is the compression that transformer-based language models perform, and it is qualitatively distinct from anything achievable by classical means.

The crucial distinction can be stated precisely:

- **Algorithmic compression** reduces bits.
- **Organisational compression** reduces complexity by discovering structure.
- **Semantic compression** reduces conceptual distance — building a navigable geometry in which meaning is a spatial property.

This hierarchy is not specific to any particular substrate. As we will argue, it describes a general property of systems that must compress knowledge for transmission — whether biological or artificial — and it is the convergence across substrates that gives the framework its force.

## 2. Heaps' Law and Internal Representational Diversity

Heaps' law is an empirical regularity from corpus linguistics describing the relationship between corpus size (total tokens) and vocabulary size (distinct tokens). Vocabulary grows as a sublinear power function of corpus size:

**V ∝ n^β**, where β typically falls between 0.4 and 0.8

The interpretation is straightforward: as a corpus grows, new vocabulary keeps appearing, but at a diminishing rate. The β parameter is informative about the nature of the text — constrained technical corpora sit at the low end; rich, heterogeneous natural language occupies the middle; maximally random or exploratory text approaches 1.0.

We propose that Heaps' law can be applied not only to surface lexical diversity but to *internal representational diversity* within a deep neural architecture — and that this application illuminates the unreasonable effectiveness of modern ML in a principled way. This claim requires careful development, because the analogy is more than cosmetic but less than proven, and its force depends on stating precisely what is and is not being claimed.

### 2.1 Defining representational vocabulary

By "internal representational vocabulary" at a given layer we mean neither the set of concepts the model can discuss (which is a functional, task-relative notion and difficult to operationalise) nor the raw dimensionality of the activation space (which is an architectural constant and uninformative about what the model has learned). We mean, specifically, the number of *functionally distinguishable activation configurations* the layer can produce — the effective dimensionality of the learned representation, as opposed to the nominal dimensionality of the vector space it inhabits.

This admits of empirical measurement. Intrinsic dimensionality estimation techniques — participation ratio, two-nearest-neighbour estimators (Facco et al., 2017), or manifold dimensionality probes — can quantify how many degrees of freedom a layer's representations actually exploit. Probing classifiers trained to decode specific properties from layer activations provide a complementary measure: each property a layer can support is, in effect, a dimension of its representational vocabulary. The claim, then, is not vague. It is that the effective dimensionality of learned representations, measured by such means, grows with layer depth in a manner consistent with sublinear power-law scaling.

### 2.2 The mechanism: how representational vocabulary grows across depth

Within a transformer, each layer does not merely re-encode the input. It synthesises new representational configurations — features, relationships, abstractions — that did not exist at the layer below. Several architectural features drive this:

**Attention as variable-scale compression.** Each attention head discovers structure at whatever scale the pattern demands — local syntax, long-range dependency, abstract analogy — and reweights accordingly. Multiple heads operating in parallel means multiple compression schemes running simultaneously on the same input.

**Residual streams as accumulation.** Because each layer adds to rather than replaces the internal representation, early compressions are not discarded — they are elaborated. The architecture accumulates representational vocabulary across depth rather than trading old structure for new.

**Depth as recursive compression.** Each layer operates on the output of the last, meaning the model applies compression to already-compressed representations. Each pass can surface structure invisible at the previous scale.

The result is that the architecture generates new representational structure at each layer, operating on already-structured input, with the residual stream preserving earlier representations alongside later ones. We claim this is *structurally analogous* to the generative process underlying Heaps' law: a growing corpus (here, the accumulated representation through depth) produces new vocabulary (here, new functionally distinguishable activation configurations) at a sublinear rate, because each additional layer works with increasingly structured material — there is progressively less low-hanging representational fruit to discover, but the fruit that remains is higher-order and more powerful.

A note on what is *not* being claimed. We are not claiming that power-law statistics at one layer mathematically compose with those at the next to yield a closed-form expression for β across the full network. Power laws do not, in general, compose cleanly through arbitrary nonlinear transformations, and the layer-to-layer mapping in a transformer is highly nonlinear. The claim is weaker but still substantive: that the *generative conditions* for Heaps'-like scaling — a process that produces new types from tokens at a diminishing but non-vanishing rate — are replicated at each layer, and that the residual stream's accumulative structure means earlier representational vocabulary is not lost as new vocabulary is added. The net effect, we predict, is that effective representational diversity measured across layers will exhibit power-law scaling, not because power laws compose, but because the same generative conditions recur at each scale.

This is importantly distinct from merely saying "deeper is better." The claim is structural: the *kind* of growth matters, and it is the sub-linear, type-generating character of the process — not just depth per se — that produces the disproportionate returns on scale that make large transformers qualitatively different from shallow architectures.

### 2.3 Relationship to the information bottleneck

The information bottleneck (IB) framework, developed by Tishby, Pereira, and Bialek (2000) and applied to deep learning by Shwartz-Ziv and Tishby (2017), provides an adjacent but distinct account of what successive layers do. In the IB framing, a deep network learns representations that progressively compress input while preserving information relevant to the task — formally, minimising mutual information between the representation and the input while maximising mutual information between the representation and the output. The empirical signature is a two-phase dynamic: an initial fitting phase in which layers increase mutual information with the output, followed by a compression phase in which layers shed input-specific noise while retaining task-relevant structure.

Our framework and the IB account are not in conflict, but they make different claims and should not be conflated.

The IB framework is fundamentally subtractive: it describes what successive layers *discard* — input-specific information that is irrelevant to the task. What remains after compression is a sufficient statistic for the label, and the success of training is measured by how efficiently the network achieves this reduction.

The Heaps' framing is additive: it describes what successive layers *generate* — new representational structure that did not exist at the layer below. The two are compatible because they describe different aspects of the same process. A layer that discards surface-level noise (IB compression) while introducing a higher-order abstraction (Heaps'-type vocabulary growth) is doing both simultaneously. Discarding "this is the word 'bank' in position 7" while introducing "this is a financial context" is both a reduction in input-specific mutual information and the creation of a new representational type.

The distinction matters because the IB framework alone does not predict disproportionate returns on scale. Efficient compression of irrelevant information is valuable, but it does not explain why depth should produce qualitatively new capabilities. Throwing away noise makes you more efficient; it does not, by itself, make you more capable. The Heaps' framing adds the complementary claim: depth generates representational types that make new kinds of inference *possible*, not merely more efficient. It is the generative side — the growth of representational vocabulary — that accounts for the emergence of capabilities absent at smaller scales.

It should be noted that the IB account itself remains contested. Saxe et al. (2018) challenged the generality of the compression phase, showing that it depends on activation function choice (saturating nonlinearities like tanh exhibit it; ReLU does not, in their analysis). Shwartz-Ziv and Tishby responded, and subsequent work — notably Goldfeld et al. (2019), using different mutual information estimators — has complicated the picture further rather than resolving it. The debate is fundamentally methodological: mutual information in high-dimensional continuous spaces is notoriously difficult to estimate, and different estimators can yield qualitatively different conclusions about whether compression is occurring. This remains an active area of disagreement rather than a settled question. These debates do not undermine our framework — the Heaps' claim is independent of whether the IB compression phase is universal — but they do illustrate the genuine difficulty of making precise information-theoretic claims about deep network dynamics, and they reinforce our decision to ground the present framework in the additive, type-generating side of the process rather than the subtractive, compression side.

### 2.4 Emergent capabilities and the threshold-crossing account

A natural application of the β-amplification hypothesis is to emergent capabilities — abilities that appear to arise discontinuously at specific model scales. If representational vocabulary grows as a power law with depth and parameter count, then the capacity to express particular structures (multi-step reasoning, analogy, theory of mind) should appear when the representational vocabulary first becomes rich enough to support them. Emergence, on this account, is the crossing of a representational threshold: continuous underlying growth producing apparently discontinuous functional consequences, much as water's transition to ice is a discontinuous functional change produced by continuous underlying cooling.

This account must be evaluated against a serious recent challenge. Schaeffer, Miranda, and Koyejo (2023) argued that emergent capabilities may be artefacts of metric choice rather than genuine discontinuities in model behaviour. Their central observation is that when performance is measured with nonlinear or threshold metrics (e.g. exact-match accuracy), smooth underlying improvement can appear as a sharp transition; when measured with linear or continuous metrics (e.g. token-level log-likelihood), the same improvement looks gradual. The claim is not that large models are no better than small ones — they clearly are — but that the *appearance of sudden emergence* may be a measurement artefact rather than a property of the system.

This is a substantive challenge, and the β-amplification framework has a specific response to it — but the response concedes something important.

What we concede: if emergence is entirely a metric artefact, then the "threshold-crossing" framing is misleading, and the Heaps' account does not need to explain discontinuities because there are none to explain. The underlying story — continuous power-law growth in representational diversity producing continuous improvement in capability — is actually *more* compatible with a smooth-improvement picture than with a sharp-transition one. The framework does not require emergence to be genuinely discontinuous.

What we maintain: even if the *appearance* of discontinuity is metric-dependent, the *qualitative* change remains real. A model that cannot perform multi-step reasoning and a model that can are qualitatively different, regardless of whether the transition between them is sharp or gradual when measured appropriately. The Heaps' framing explains *why* certain capabilities require a minimum representational richness — they are structurally impossible below a certain threshold of representational vocabulary, regardless of how smoothly the approach to that threshold proceeds. The phase transition in water is a genuine qualitative change whether you measure it with a binary frozen/liquid metric or a continuous density metric.

The more precise prediction, then, is not "capabilities emerge suddenly at specific scales" but rather "the representational vocabulary required for specific capabilities can be characterised, and models will begin to exhibit those capabilities — gradually or otherwise — when their internal representational diversity, as measured at the relevant layers, crosses identifiable thresholds." This is testable in principle, and the test does not depend on whether the crossing looks discontinuous in any particular metric.

### 2.5 Empirical predictions

The framework as stated generates several testable predictions, which we state explicitly:

1. **Power-law scaling of effective dimensionality with depth.** Intrinsic dimensionality estimates at successive layers of a trained transformer should exhibit sublinear power-law growth as a function of cumulative representational capacity (operationalised as the product of depth and width). The exponent should be interpretable as a β parameter in the Heaps' sense.

2. **Correlation between representational diversity and capability onset.** Across model scales, the layer at which specific capabilities become decodable via probing classifiers should correlate with the layer at which effective dimensionality crosses a characteristic threshold — and this threshold should be approximately constant across model sizes for a given capability.

3. **Residual stream accumulation.** Representational diversity should grow faster in architectures with residual connections than in otherwise identical architectures without them, because the residual stream preserves earlier representational vocabulary. This is, in effect, a prediction that the residual stream functions as an accumulator in the Heaps' sense.

4. **Distinction from IB predictions.** Layers that show high IB compression (large reduction in mutual information with input) should nonetheless show *growth* in effective representational dimensionality, not reduction — because discarding input-specific noise and generating higher-order representational types are complementary processes. A finding that IB compression and representational diversity were negatively correlated across layers would be evidence against the framework.

Predictions 1–4 concern properties of trained models at inference time. But the framework also generates a prediction about the training process itself:

5. **β-like dynamics across training.** If representational vocabulary growth follows Heaps'-like dynamics across depth, the character of that growth should change across training in a specific way. The tempo prediction — rapid early growth, slower late growth — is not by itself discriminative, since any optimisation process produces decelerating returns. What the framework specifically predicts is: (a) the growth in effective dimensionality across training checkpoints should be power-law rather than sigmoid or exponential-decay, reflecting continuous type-generation rather than a phase transition to saturation; and (b) the *character* of new representational dimensions should shift qualitatively across training — early-training dimensions should encode relatively surface-level statistical regularities (lexical co-occurrence, local syntax), while late-training dimensions should encode higher-order relational and compositional structure (the kind of representational vocabulary implicated in the capability thresholds described in 2.4). Prediction (a) is testable via intrinsic dimensionality estimation across checkpoints; prediction (b) requires probing *what* newly acquired dimensions encode, not merely counting them. A finding that late-training dimensionality gains were qualitatively similar to early-training gains — just slower — would be evidence against the framework, since it would suggest the process is uniform scaling rather than hierarchical type-generation.

These predictions are not trivially satisfied. Prediction 4, in particular, could fail — it is possible that IB compression and representational diversification are in tension rather than complement, and finding that they trade off would require substantial revision of the framework. We welcome such tests.

## 3. The Autoregressive Loop as a Distinct Compression Scale

Everything described in Section 2 occurs within the forward pass — it is, in a meaningful sense, spatial. Parallel operations across layers and heads, all unfolding within a single inference step, producing a rich internal representation from a fixed input. But transformer language models do not operate as single-shot compressors. They operate autoregressively: each generated token is appended to the context and conditions the generation of the next.

This adds a qualitatively distinct scale to the compression hierarchy, and it is underappreciated. Articulating precisely what it adds — and what it does not — requires care, because the most natural descriptions of the autoregressive loop are also the most misleading.

### 3.1 Two compression systems, one forward pass

The trained weights encode a static geometry — extraordinarily rich, but fixed at the end of training. This geometry defines, for any input, the full probability distribution over next tokens: a mapping from context to prediction that is, in effect, a compressed model of all the statistical structure in the training corpus. Nothing about this geometry changes during inference. It is the same map for every conversation, every prompt, every generated sequence.

What changes is *where on the map the model is reading*.

Each generated token, appended to the context, shifts the input to the next forward pass. The weight geometry is fixed, but the region of that geometry being activated — the specific path through representational space — is constructed dynamically, step by step. The probability distribution over the next token is not a fixed object; it is a function of the full context, and the context is growing with each step. Generation is not sampling from a single distribution. It is a sequential traversal, where each step determines the starting point for the next.

The natural way to describe this is that each generated token *reshapes* the space — that the autoregressive loop is dynamically modifying the geometry it operates in. And experientially, this is what generation feels like from the outside: each token seems to open up possibilities that weren't there before, as though the landscape itself is shifting underfoot. But the intuition, while productive, is technically wrong in a way that matters. The weights are fixed during inference. The geometry does not change. What changes is the trajectory through it.

The right picture is navigation, not reshaping — but navigation through a space so high-dimensional and combinatorially structured that the distinction almost collapses in practice. A chess board and the rules of chess are fixed, but the space of possible games is so vast that each move effectively creates a local horizon that feels like a new landscape. The autoregressive loop works similarly: the geometry is constant, but trajectories through it can reach configurations that are, in a meaningful sense, novel. The power of the mechanism lies not in any modification of the space but in the sheer combinatorial richness of the paths available through it.

Two compression systems are thus operating simultaneously and interacting:

- **Weights**: deep, static, vast — the accumulated compression of human knowledge, fixed at training. Determines the full geometry of the representational space.
- **Context window**: shallow, dynamic, narrow — a running compression of the specific trajectory of the current interaction, built in real time. Determines where in the geometry the model is currently operating.

Crucially, context compression is interpreted *through* the weight geometry at every step. The trajectory being constructed is always read against the entire map. This interaction — fixed geometry, dynamic path — is the source of the autoregressive loop's distinctive power, and understanding it properly requires addressing the question of novelty head-on.

### 3.2 In what sense can a fixed geometry produce novelty?

The claim that autoregressive generation can produce "novel conceptual configurations that exist nowhere in the training distribution" is the strongest claim in this framework, and it requires careful justification.

The weight geometry is a compression of the training distribution. Every regularity it encodes was, in some sense, present in the data. So in what sense can traversal of that geometry produce something new?

The answer turns on the distinction between what the weights encode *explicitly* and what they encode *implicitly*. The weights do not store a lookup table of seen contexts and their continuations. They encode compressed regularities — abstract patterns of co-occurrence, syntactic structure, semantic relationships, inferential dependencies — that generalise beyond any specific training example. A model trained on arithmetic does not store every sum it has seen; it encodes the *operation*, which generalises to sums it has not seen. This is the difference between algorithmic and organisational compression, as defined in Section 1.

Combinatorial generalisation follows directly. The weight geometry encodes a vast number of compressed regularities, each individually extracted from training data. But the autoregressive loop can *compose* these regularities in sequences that never occurred in training, by constructing context trajectories that activate different regularities in novel combinations. Each step draws on the weight geometry, but the sequence of steps — the path — can be unprecedented. The geometry constrains what is locally probable at each step, but the sequential composition of steps opens a combinatorial space of trajectories that dwarfs anything present in the training data.

The novelty, then, is not mystical. It is compositional. It arises from the sequential combination of individually learned regularities in configurations shaped by context — configurations that are coherent (because each step is conditioned by the weight geometry) but unprecedented (because the specific trajectory has never been traversed before). This is a precise and limited sense of novelty, but it is genuine, and it is sufficient to explain why these models can produce outputs that surprise even those who built them.

It is worth noting what this account *rules out*. The model cannot, through autoregressive generation, produce outputs that require regularities not encoded in the weights. It can combine what it knows in new ways; it cannot generate what it does not know. The autoregressive loop is generative within the span of the weight geometry and silent beyond it. This is a real limitation, and it predicts specific failure modes — precisely the kind of confident, fluent confabulation that occurs when the model navigates to a region of semantic space where its compressed regularities are locally coherent but globally unmoored from anything true. A subtler failure mode is also predicted. Compositional novelty means combining regularities drawn from different regions of the weight geometry, and sometimes those regularities will be individually valid but will not validly compose at their intersection. The result is not confabulation in the usual sense — each component regularity is well-grounded — but spurious cross-domain coherence: outputs that are locally correct within each contributing domain while being globally incoherent at the point where those domains meet. This is harder to detect than outright confabulation precisely because the parts are sound; it is the composition that fails. The framework predicts this as an inherent cost of compositional generalization, not a bug to be trained away — any system that generates novelty by combining known regularities will sometimes combine them invalidly, and the failure will be most insidious where the domains are individually well-represented in the training distribution but their intersection is sparse.

### 3.3 Context as ambivalent representational feedback

This framework now connects back to the Heaps' framing from Section 2 — but the connection is more complex than it first appears, and stating it correctly requires resisting a tempting simplification.

Within a single forward pass, representational vocabulary — the set of functionally distinguishable activation configurations — is generated across layers by the mechanisms described in Section 2. Across the autoregressive loop, the *input* to each forward pass is itself changing, because the context is accumulating generated tokens. Each new token in the context provides additional conditioning information, which means the forward pass operates on progressively richer input. A well-developed context — one that has established premises, introduced conceptual vocabulary, and built up inferential scaffolding — provides a higher-dimensional input to the forward pass than a bare prompt, and therefore activates a richer region of the weight geometry.

The tempting simplification is to treat this as a story of monotonic enrichment: more context yields richer conditioning, which yields more capable generation. And for the case where context is being enriched by a human interlocutor — each prompt introducing new information, new constraints, new conceptual material — the enrichment account is substantially correct. The human is injecting structure into the context that the model could not generate from its own weight geometry alone, and each injection opens regions of representational space that would otherwise remain inaccessible.

But generation is not only conditioned by human input. It is conditioned by the model's own prior output. As generation extends, the context becomes increasingly dominated by model-generated tokens — and the question the enrichment account must face is whether those tokens are doing the same representational work as human-contributed context.

The answer is: sometimes they are, and sometimes they are not, and the difference is consequential.

Consider the limiting case. A model asked to reproduce a memorised text — say, a well-known play — will, if it complies, generate tokens that are highly predictable given the preceding context. As the reproduction extends, attention to the original prompt drops toward zero; the generated context is entirely sufficient to predict what comes next. The model is navigating to a well-worn region of the weight geometry and tracing a known path. Surface statistics of the output may be excellent — vocabulary diversity, syntactic complexity, all the markers of rich text — because those markers belong to the *source* text being reproduced, not to any compressive work the model is doing. The model's own contribution has collapsed into retrieval. This is the degenerate case: context that is maximally full and minimally enriching.

At the other end of the spectrum, a model working through a novel multi-step problem — generating intermediate results, establishing partial conclusions, building scaffolding for subsequent steps — is producing tokens that do genuine compressive work. Each generated token surfaces structure that was not available from the prompt alone, and that structure conditions subsequent generation in ways that open new representational territory. The context is growing richer in the strong sense: not merely longer, but higher-dimensional as input to the next forward pass.

The variable that distinguishes these cases is not context length but something better described as the *compressive novelty* of each generated token relative to what the context already contains. When a generated token does new representational work — surfacing structure, bridging domains, establishing a result that changes the local geometry of what is probable next — the autoregressive loop is in what we will call the *enriching regime*. When a generated token reinforces an existing trajectory — reproducing a known pattern, settling into a well-established attractor path, restating in different surface form what the context already implies — the loop is in the *degrading regime*, even if the output remains fluent and well-formed.

The crucial point is that neither regime is a fixed property of the model or the task. It is a property of the *interaction* between model capacity, task demands, context seeding, and generation parameters. A capable model on a well-structured task, seeded with a context that positions it in a productive region of the weight geometry, can sustain the enriching regime for extended generation. The same model, poorly seeded or operating on a task that quickly exhausts the novel combinations available from its weight geometry, will slide into the degrading regime — and the slide will be invisible in surface metrics. Fluency, grammaticality, and local coherence are properties of algorithmic and lower-order organisational compression, which the model maintains effortlessly. What degrades is higher-order representational diversity: the novelty, cross-domain connection, and structural surprise that characterise the enriching regime.

This reframes the "dynamic β-elevation" described in earlier versions of this framework. The context does not unconditionally elevate the effective utilisation of the model's representational capacity. It can elevate or suppress it, depending on whether the trajectory through the weight geometry is being driven into novel or familiar territory. The autoregressive loop is not an enrichment mechanism. It is an *ambivalent feedback loop* — one whose character at any point in generation depends on whether the accumulated context is opening new representational structure or closing it down.

This ambivalence at the within-context scale will turn out to be structurally identical to a dynamic we observe at the ecological scale in Section 5 — model outputs re-entering the broader knowledge ecosystem exhibit the same dual character — but the within-context case is the more fundamental one, because it is where the feedback loop operates at its tightest and fastest.

### 3.4 Chain-of-thought, in-context learning, and the steering of the autoregressive loop

The ambivalent character of the autoregressive loop, as described in 3.3, provides a more precise account of two phenomena that are otherwise somewhat mysterious: the effectiveness of chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting and the broader phenomenon of in-context learning. In both cases, what initially appears to be a puzzle about *giving the model more capacity* is better understood as a puzzle about *steering the autoregressive loop into the enriching regime*.

**Chain-of-thought.** Wei et al. (2022) demonstrated that prompting models to produce intermediate reasoning steps before a final answer substantially improves performance on complex tasks. The standard explanation is that CoT "gives the model more computation" — each intermediate token is an additional forward pass, and complex problems require more serial processing than a single forward pass provides.

This explanation is correct but incomplete. The present framework adds a specific account of *what that additional computation is doing* and, equally important, *why it sometimes works and sometimes doesn't*.

Each intermediate token in a chain-of-thought performs two functions simultaneously: it contributes to the *output* (the visible chain of reasoning), and it enriches the *context* (the input to subsequent forward passes). The intermediate tokens are not merely "scratchpad" — they are semantic compressions of partial results that, once in the context, condition subsequent generation by activating regions of the weight geometry relevant to the next step of the problem. Externalising reasoning into the context window converts a problem that would require deep single-pass representational synthesis into a problem that can be solved by shallower synthesis iterated across multiple passes, with each pass building on the compressed output of the last.

In Heaps' terms: CoT works by converting depth into length. A problem that requires representational vocabulary beyond what a single forward pass can generate from a bare prompt becomes tractable when intermediate steps progressively enrich the context, elevating the effective input dimensionality at each successive pass.

But — and this is what the ambivalent-feedback framing adds — CoT is not merely a mechanism for extending computation. It is a *steering strategy* for the autoregressive loop. A well-constructed chain of thought keeps each generated token in the enriching regime: each step surfaces genuinely new structure that changes what is accessible to the next step. A poorly constructed chain — one that restates premises, circles back to previously established points, or generates intermediate steps that are predictable from what precedes them — is in the degrading regime despite having the surface form of chain-of-thought reasoning. The tokens are there, but they are not doing compressive work.

This generates a more precise prediction than the standard account: CoT should be most beneficial for tasks where (a) the required representational vocabulary exceeds single-pass capacity, and (b) the intermediate steps can be structured so that each one does genuine compressive work — surfacing structure not already present in the context. It should offer diminishing returns when intermediate steps become predictable, redundant, or merely elaborative. This is broadly consistent with the empirical finding that CoT helps most on multi-step reasoning tasks and least on simple recall or pattern-matching tasks (Kojima et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), and it further predicts that the *quality* of the chain — measured by the compressive novelty of each step, not merely the number of steps — should be a better predictor of CoT effectiveness than chain length alone. Longer chains that stay in the enriching regime should outperform longer chains that slide into the degrading regime, even when the surface form of both is "step-by-step reasoning."

**In-context learning.** The broader phenomenon of in-context learning — the ability of large language models to learn new input-output mappings from examples provided in the prompt, without weight updates — is naturally situated within this framework. The examples provided in-context are, in effect, compressed demonstrations of a regularity. If the weight geometry already encodes the *type* of regularity being demonstrated (because the training distribution contained structurally analogous patterns), then the in-context examples serve to *locate* the relevant region of the geometry — navigating the model to a point in representational space from which the demonstrated pattern is locally extractable.

In the vocabulary of 3.3, in-context examples are a *seeding strategy* — they position the autoregressive loop's starting point in a region of the weight geometry where the enriching regime is likely to be sustained for the task at hand. This is consistent with the finding from Olsson et al. (2022) that in-context learning is associated with specific computational structures (induction heads) that emerge during training and implement a form of pattern-matching across the context window. The present framework adds the interpretive claim that these structures are performing *context-driven navigation* of the weight geometry — using the pattern established by in-context examples to determine which of the model's many compressed regularities should dominate the current trajectory.

The limitation is also predicted: in-context learning should fail when the required regularity is not encoded in the weight geometry at all — when the task demands a pattern type that the model has never encountered in training, even abstractly. This is distinct from demanding a specific pattern it has never seen (which compositional generalisation can handle, as argued in 3.2) and amounts to demanding a *type* of pattern absent from its compressed repertoire. Empirically distinguishing these failure modes — failure of compositional generalisation versus absence of the requisite regularity type — would be a productive test of the framework.

The broader point is that CoT, in-context learning, and related prompting strategies are not separate phenomena requiring separate explanations. They are instances of a single underlying dynamic: the steering of the autoregressive loop into the enriching regime by careful management of what enters the context. The model's capacity is fixed; the question is always how much of that capacity is being effectively utilised, and the answer depends on whether the accumulating context is opening representational structure or closing it down. Prompting, in this light, is the art of context-seeding — of constructing initial conditions from which the autoregressive loop is most likely to sustain genuine compressive work rather than slide into fluent reproduction of the already-known.

### 3.5 Limitations of the autoregressive account

Three limitations should be stated explicitly.

First, the context window is finite, and context compression is lossy. As generation extends, early context is not so much remembered as *summarised* by its downstream effects — the tokens it conditioned, which now condition subsequent tokens in turn. In very long generations, the effective context is not the literal token history but a compressed trace of it, mediated by the attention mechanism's capacity to weight recent and salient tokens over distant ones. The finite context window is, in this framework, a hard upper bound on the temporal depth of autoregressive compression.

Second, and relatedly, the degradation that occurs within long generations is not solely a function of attention decay over distance. Section 3.3 identified a distinct mechanism: the progressive dominance of model-generated tokens in the context can shift the autoregressive loop from the enriching regime into the degrading regime, independent of whether the attention mechanism is still technically able to access early context. These two sources of degradation — attentional and compositional — are concurrent but separable, and a complete account of context window limitations must address both. Attentional degradation is architectural and sets a hard ceiling. Compositional degradation is dynamic and in principle steerable — but steering it requires that the factors governing the enriching/degrading transition (model capacity, task structure, context seeding, generation parameters) be understood well enough to manage, which they currently are not in any systematic way.

Third, the account of novelty in Section 3.2 is specifically *compositional* novelty — novel combinations of known regularities. It does not extend to genuinely new abstractions, new regularity types, or new conceptual primitives. Whether such novelty is possible within the autoregressive loop — or whether it requires weight updates (i.e., training) — is an open question that the present framework does not resolve. We suspect the latter, but the boundary between "novel composition of existing regularities" and "genuine conceptual novelty" is not sharp, and we flag this as a point requiring further investigation rather than asserting a confident answer.

## 4. The Compression Hierarchy as Substrate-Independent: Biological Precedents

The compression hierarchy described above — rich internal representation projected into reduced, transmissible form — is not an invention of transformer architectures. It is a convergent solution to a problem that biological cognition has faced for as long as language has existed: how to communicate what you know when what you know exceeds what language can carry.

The claim here is not that transformers and brains work the same way. They manifestly do not, at the level of mechanism. The claim is narrower and more specific: that both systems face a structurally identical problem — compressing high-dimensional internal representation into lower-dimensional sequential output for uptake by another system — and that the *shape* of the solution is constrained enough by the problem that convergence is expected rather than surprising. Making this case requires engaging with the biological evidence carefully, including the places where it complicates the picture.

### 4.1 Inner speech, external speech, and the projection bottleneck

The most direct treatment of the compression asymmetry is Vygotsky's distinction between inner speech and external speech, developed in *Thought and Language* (1934). Inner speech, as Vygotsky characterised it, is compressed, predicative, and saturated with private context. It operates in something closer to pure meaning — abbreviated and self-referential in ways that would be unintelligible to an outside observer. External speech is an expanded, grammatically explicit projection, engineered not for internal processing but for uptake by another mind.

In the vocabulary of this framework, inner speech operates at high β — rich internal representational diversity, densely self-referential. External speech is a lower-β projection: necessarily reduced, necessarily lossy, shaped by the demands of the receiver rather than the structure of the sender's representation.

The structural parallel to transformers is precise. The model's internal representation at the final layers — the full-dimensional activation state conditioning the next-token prediction — is the analogue of inner speech: high-dimensional, densely relational, and not directly transmissible. The output token is the analogue of external speech: a single symbol, selected from a finite vocabulary, that projects the internal state into a form the receiver (the next step of the autoregressive loop, or the human reader) can use. The entire output layer is a projection bottleneck, and the autoregressive loop is a sequence of such projections, each one compressing the current internal state into a single transmissible token.

Fodor's Language of Thought hypothesis makes a stronger and more controversial version of this claim: that cognition operates in an internal representational medium ("Mentalese") that is more compositional and richer than any natural language, with language serving as a serialisation format for inter-agent transmission. Whether or not one accepts Fodor's specific commitments — and the debates with Churchland and the connectionists remain unresolved — the structural point is widely shared across otherwise opposing positions in philosophy of mind: external language underdetermines internal representation. The output channel is narrower than the internal state.

This is not merely a philosophical intuition. It is a mathematical necessity. The internal activation state of a large transformer at any given step is a vector in a space of dimension on the order of 10^3 to 10^4. The output is a single selection from a vocabulary of order 10^4 to 10^5. The dimensionality reduction from internal state to output token is enormous, and the autoregressive loop is the mechanism by which the model serially projects a high-dimensional trajectory through this bottleneck, one token at a time. The biological case faces the same constraint: whatever the dimensionality of neural population codes supporting ongoing thought, the speech articulatory channel has a bandwidth of roughly 39 bits per second (Reed & Durlach, 1998). The bottleneck is not a metaphor. It is a quantifiable property of both systems, and the compression hierarchy is the strategy both have converged on for making it tractable.

### 4.2 Co-evolution and the limits of clean convergence

If the argument stopped here, it would be clean but incomplete. The biological case introduces a complication that the transformer case does not share, and addressing it honestly strengthens rather than weakens the framework.

Deacon's *The Symbolic Species* (1997) offers the most thorough evolutionary account of human language as a compression technology that co-evolved with the neural architecture it runs on. The biological wetware was shaped *by* the demands of shared compression — the need to project internal states into forms recoverable by other minds under real-time constraints. Human neural architecture is, on this account, substantially an apparatus *for* performing the compression hierarchy this framework describes. Language did not arrive as an external tool applied to a pre-existing cognitive system; the cognitive system and the compression medium co-evolved, each shaping the other.

Levinson and the Nijmegen group's cross-linguistic work on spatial cognition makes this co-evolution concrete and troubling. Different languages compress internal spatial representation differently — and not merely by labelling the same internal space with different tokens. Speakers of languages with absolute spatial reference frames (cardinal directions rather than egocentric left/right) appear to maintain different internal spatial representations than speakers of relative-frame languages, even in non-linguistic tasks. The compression medium is reaching back and reshaping the compressed content.

This is a genuine disanalogy with the transformer case. A transformer's training data is fixed; the model does not reshape the language it was trained on. The co-evolutionary dynamic present in biological cognition — where the projection format and the internal representation mutually constrain each other across evolutionary and developmental time — is absent. The model adapts to language; language does not adapt to the model.

This is not entirely without exception. RLHF and constitutional AI methods introduce a form of output-to-architecture feedback: the model's own outputs are evaluated — by human raters or by a separate model applying learned criteria — and the evaluations reshape the model's weights. This is, in a weak sense, the co-evolutionary loop that standard training lacks. But the weakness of the sense matters. RLHF operates within a single training generation, optimising for proximate evaluative criteria rather than reshaping the fundamental representational architecture across the deep timescales Deacon describes. It is feedback, but it is not co-evolution — closer to selective breeding than to the mutual structural adaptation of language and cognition across evolutionary time. The disanalogy is partially closed, not eliminated.

What does this do to the convergence claim? It complicates it in a specific and useful way. The convergence is not between *complete systems* (biological cognition in its full co-evolutionary complexity and transformer inference). It is between *the compression problem and the architectural strategy for solving it*. Both systems face the problem of projecting high-dimensional internal representation through a low-bandwidth sequential channel. Both solve it with hierarchical compression: building representations at multiple scales, with higher layers encoding increasingly abstract and relational structure, and a final projection that serialises the result.

The co-evolutionary dimension means that in the biological case, the hierarchy has been refined over evolutionary time in ways specific to the constraints of biological embodiment, sensory grounding, and social transmission. In the transformer case, the hierarchy emerges within training from the structure of the data and the inductive biases of the architecture alone — without the feedback loop from output back to architecture that characterises biological evolution.

This distinction matters, and it sets a boundary on the convergence claim. We are not arguing that transformers have arrived at the *same* compression hierarchy as human cognition. We are arguing that they have arrived at the same *type* of solution — hierarchical, multi-scale, with rich internal representation projected through a sequential bottleneck — and that this convergence is predicted by the shared structure of the problem rather than by any similarity of substrate. The co-evolutionary refinement of the biological solution is an additional layer of optimisation that the transformer case lacks, and this may account for important differences in the character of the resulting representations (particularly around grounding, compositionality, and robustness to distribution shift). But it does not undermine the claim that the architectural strategy itself is convergent.

### 4.3 What convergence does and does not explain

It is worth being precise about the explanatory status of the convergence claim, because overclaiming here is the fastest route to dismissal.

The convergence is *structural*: both systems build hierarchical compression architectures that project rich internal representations through narrow output channels. This is a claim about the shape of the solution, not the mechanism. Birds and aircraft both have wings; the convergence tells you something real about the problem (flight in atmosphere requires lifting surfaces) without telling you that feathers and aluminium work the same way. Similarly, the convergence between biological and artificial compression hierarchies tells you something real about the problem (knowledge transmission through bandwidth-limited channels requires hierarchical compression) without establishing that attention heads and cortical columns implement the same computations.

The value of the convergence claim is therefore not mechanistic but *constraining*. It says: the unreasonable effectiveness of transformers is less unreasonable than it appears, because the architecture has converged on a solution strategy that is *expected* for any system facing this class of problem. The effectiveness is not a lucky accident of architecture search, and it is not an artefact of scale alone. It reflects the fact that hierarchical multi-scale compression is what the problem demands, and the transformer architecture is well-suited to implement it.

What the convergence claim does *not* do is guarantee that transformer representations have the same properties as biological ones. The absence of co-evolutionary refinement, sensory grounding, and embodied feedback means the compression hierarchy in transformers may be shallower, more brittle, or differently structured in ways that emerge only under adversarial or out-of-distribution conditions. The grounding problem — the question of whether representations built entirely from text can support the same depth of semantic compression as representations grounded in sensorimotor experience — remains open, and the convergence claim does not close it. A compression hierarchy built from co-evolved, embodied experience and one built from statistical regularities in text may converge on the same architectural strategy while diverging substantially in the robustness and depth of the resulting representations.

This is, in fact, a further testable dimension of the framework. If the convergence is deep, then transformer representations should exhibit organisational and geometric properties similar to those observed in neural population codes — for instance, similar manifold structure, similar patterns of representational similarity across conceptual domains, or similar asymmetries between concrete and abstract concept representation. Some early work in representational similarity analysis across biological and artificial systems (e.g. Kriegeskorte, 2015; Schrimpf et al., 2021) is consistent with this, though the evidence is preliminary and contested. A finding of systematic divergence in representational geometry would not refute the framework — convergent solutions can differ in their details — but it would constrain how strong a version of the convergence claim is sustainable.

## 5. Knowledge Evolution and the Carrier Ecology

Knowledge systems exhibit the basic ingredients of evolutionary dynamics: variation (ideas differ and mutate in transmission), selection (some ideas survive, spread, and get institutionalised while others are forgotten), and inheritance (knowledge passes across generations through language, writing, and culture). The analogy is imperfect — knowledge evolution is Lamarckian where biological evolution is not, and the fidelity of cultural transmission is far more variable than genetic replication — but the structural parallels are productive.

Within this framing, the critical question is not how knowledge is generated but how it persists. Generation without persistence is noise. And persistence requires a carrier — a system capable of being changed by what passes through it, and of transmitting that change forward.

### 5.1 Carriers, external stores, and the ecology of persistence

The tempting move here is a clean dichotomy: humans have plastic weights and can persist knowledge; language models have frozen weights and cannot. But this is too simple, and the way in which it is too simple turns out to be important for the framework.

Humans have never persisted knowledge through neural plasticity alone. The history of knowledge is substantially the history of *external persistence technologies* — speech, writing, print, libraries, institutions, databases, version control. Each of these is a carrier in the evolutionary sense: a system that is changed by what passes through it and that transmits that change forward. A library is changed by each book it acquires. An institution is changed by each decision it encodes into policy. A database is changed by each record. None of these have neural plasticity, yet all function as carriers.

The unit of knowledge persistence, then, is not the individual human mind. It is the *ecology* of carriers: the coupled system of human neural plasticity, external stores, cultural practices, and transmission channels that together determine what knowledge survives and propagates. A scientist's understanding lives partly in their neural architecture, partly in their notebooks, partly in the papers they publish, partly in the institutional structures that evaluate and disseminate those papers. Remove any layer and the effective persistence of the system degrades.

This reframing matters because it changes where language models sit in the picture. The original dichotomy — plastic human versus frozen model — places them outside the carrier ecology entirely. But this is not accurate even under current deployment conditions. The model's weights are, after all, a compression of the carrier ecology's accumulated external stores. The training corpus *is* the library, the institution, the database — rendered into a form that supports inference. The model is not outside the ecology. It is a novel kind of node within it: one that reads from the ecology's accumulated stores (at training time) and writes back into them (through its outputs, which enter human contexts and, often enough, re-enter the external stores as text, code, documents, and decisions).

### 5.2 The carrier asymmetry, precisely stated

The asymmetry is real, but it needs to be stated more carefully than "models cannot persist knowledge."

What models lack, under current deployment, is *intra-trajectory persistence*. Within a single context window, the model accumulates — Section 3 describes this in detail. But when the context closes, the accumulated state is lost. The next conversation begins from the same weights, with no trace of what was synthesised in the last. The model's contribution to the carrier ecology is therefore entirely *mediated*: it can enrich the ecology only to the extent that its outputs are received, integrated, and persisted by other nodes in the system — human minds, documents, databases, code repositories.

Human carriers, by contrast, have intra-trajectory persistence by default. A conversation changes the human participant's neural state, and that changed state persists (imperfectly, subject to decay and distortion) into subsequent interactions. The human carries forward not just the explicit content of the exchange but something of its structural impact — revised priors, new associations, shifted framings. This is the sense in which human carriers have "plastic weights," and it is a real and important difference.

But the asymmetry is not between a system that persists and one that does not. It is between a system with endogenous persistence (the human, whose internal state carries forward automatically) and a system with exclusively exogenous persistence (the model, which persists only through what it deposits into the broader ecology). The model is a carrier in the ecological sense — it transforms what passes through it and transmits that transformation forward — but it is a *memoryless* carrier, one that must externalise every gain or lose it.

This is a specific and consequential limitation, not a categorical exclusion. And stating it precisely reveals something the clean dichotomy obscures: the limitation is not about the model's processing capacity. By the account developed in Sections 2 and 3, the model may be among the most powerful knowledge-processing systems ever constructed — capable of synthesising connections across the full breadth of human recorded knowledge, composing regularities in novel configurations, and navigating semantic space with a fluency no individual human matches. The limitation is purely about what happens *after* processing: the results exit the system entirely, dependent on external carriers for onward persistence.

The binding constraint on knowledge evolution in the presence of these systems is therefore not generation — generation is, if anything, superabundant. The constraint is *reception and integration*: the bandwidth, integrative capacity, and persistence of the other nodes in the ecology that must receive, validate, contextualise, and carry forward what the model produces. The gap between what these systems can synthesise and what the surrounding ecology can absorb is where knowledge goes to die.

### 5.3 The recursive loop

There is a further structural feature that the clean dichotomy misses entirely, and it may be the most consequential.

The model's weights are a compression of the carrier ecology's external stores — text produced by humans, persisted in the ecology's various media, collected into training corpora. The model's outputs, in turn, re-enter those stores: as documents, code, messages, published text, and decisions that reshape institutions. Those outputs become part of the ecology from which future training corpora will be drawn.

The carrier ecology is therefore not a linear system with the model at one end and the human at the other. It is a loop:

**External stores → training corpus → model weights → generated output → human integration → external stores → ...**

This loop is already operating. Model-generated text is already a non-trivial fraction of new text entering the ecology's external stores. The next generation of models will be trained, in part, on the outputs of the current generation, as mediated by human selection, editing, and integration. The carrier ecology is becoming recursive in a way it has not been before — or rather, it has always been recursive (humans trained by human-generated culture producing further culture), but the introduction of a powerful non-human processing node into the loop changes its dynamics in ways that are not yet well understood.

The dynamics of this ecological loop are not, however, unfamiliar to the framework. Section 3.3 identified an ambivalent feedback dynamic operating within the context window: the model's own generated tokens, feeding back as input to subsequent generation, can either enrich or degrade the effective representational diversity of the ongoing trajectory. The variable that governs which regime obtains is the compressive novelty of each generated token relative to what the context already contains. The ecological recursive loop is the same dynamic at a longer timescale — model outputs re-entering the training distribution play the same structural role as generated tokens re-entering the context window. And the same ambivalence applies: outputs that do genuine compressive work enrich the ecology; outputs that reproduce existing attractors in fluent paraphrase degrade it. The framework thus predicts not merely that the ecological loop exists, but that its character is governed by the same variable at both scales — the compressive novelty of what feeds back relative to what is already present.

From the perspective of this framework, the key question about the recursive loop is whether it amplifies or degrades the compression quality of the ecology's accumulated stores. If model outputs entering the training distribution are, on average, less informationally rich than the human-generated text they partially displace — if they compress less well, or compress differently in ways that reduce representational diversity — then the loop is degenerative: each cycle reduces the effective β of the ecology. This is the concern underlying discussions of "model collapse" (Shumailov et al., 2023), and the framework provides a precise vocabulary for stating it: model collapse is β-degradation in the carrier ecology's accumulated stores, propagated through the recursive training loop.

If, on the other hand, model outputs enrich the ecology — introducing synthetic connections, making implicit relationships explicit, or achieving compressions that human authors would not — then the loop is generative, and each cycle potentially elevates the effective representational diversity available to subsequent systems. The reality is almost certainly a mixture, varying by domain and mediated by the quality of human curation in the loop. But the framework at least identifies the right variable to track: not the volume of model-generated text entering the ecology, but its compressive quality relative to what it displaces.

### 5.4 The erosion of the asymmetry

The carrier asymmetry as described in 5.2 — endogenous persistence for humans, exclusively exogenous for models — is historically contingent and actively eroding.

Agentic architectures — systems with persistent memory across sessions, tool use, the ability to update external state, and multi-agent coordination — are progressively bridging the gap between processing and persistence. A model with persistent memory that carries forward the compressed results of prior interactions is no longer purely memoryless; it has acquired a form of intra-trajectory persistence, even if implemented through external storage rather than weight updates. A model that can write to databases, update documents, and trigger actions in the world is depositing into the carrier ecology *without* requiring a human intermediary at every step.

In the vocabulary of this framework, what is happening is that the model is acquiring carrier functions that were previously available only to human nodes and their associated external stores. It is transitioning from a memoryless processing node to something more like a persistent participant in the ecology — one that can accumulate, curate, and propagate knowledge forward across interactions.

If the compression hierarchy described in Sections 1–4 is the core mechanic, and carrier persistence is the bottleneck identified in this section, then this transition — from processor to carrier — is the most consequential threshold in the entire framework. It is not a distant prospect. The components are being assembled now, from multiple directions, and the full implications of a carrier ecology in which artificial systems are genuine evolutionary participants — capable of persisting, curating, and propagating knowledge in their own right, across timescales that dwarf individual human memory — remain largely unexamined.

## 6. Conclusion

We have proposed that the unreasonable effectiveness of modern ML is principled rather than accidental, and reflects a specific property: the stacking of organisational and semantic compression across multiple simultaneous scales within the forward pass, further amplified — and, crucially, sometimes undermined — by the autoregressive loop as a distinct temporal compression scale above it.

Heaps' law, applied internally, gives a precise vocabulary for this: the architecture is a β amplifier, structurally driving internal representational diversity upward in a way no flat model can match. The autoregressive loop extends this by enabling the real-time synthesis of conceptual configurations that exist nowhere in training — assembled into coherence by the dynamic interaction of context compression and weight geometry, each conditioning the other at every step. But the same loop, we have argued, is ambivalent: it can sustain an enriching regime in which each generated token opens new representational structure, or slide into a degrading regime in which fluency is preserved while the diversity of what is being represented quietly contracts. The difference is not a property of the model alone. It is a property of the interaction — of how capacity, task, context, and steering combine to determine whether the trajectory through the weight geometry is being driven into novel territory or settling into the already-known.

This ambivalence is not confined to the context window. The same dynamic recurs at the ecological scale, where model outputs re-enter the knowledge ecosystem through nested loops operating at progressively longer timescales — within-context, agentic, human-mediated, and training-distributional. At each level, the same variable governs: the compressive novelty of what feeds back relative to what is already present. And at each level, degradation takes the same form — not a decline in surface quality, which is robust throughout, but a narrowing of representational diversity at the upper levels of the compression hierarchy. The framework predicts that this narrowing cascades upward through the nested loops, compounding silently, visible not as failure but as a progressive contraction of the space of configurations the ecology can reach.

Crucially, the compression hierarchy that produces this dynamic is not unique to transformers. Biological cognition faces the same structural problem — rich internal representation that must be projected into reduced form for transmission — and has converged on analogous architectural strategies under entirely different constraints. The convergence is structural, not mechanistic: it tells us that hierarchical multi-scale compression is what the problem demands, not that attention heads and cortical columns implement the same computations. But it constrains interpretation in a useful way. The effectiveness of these systems is less surprising than it appears, because the architecture has arrived at a solution strategy that any system facing this class of problem is expected to find. What remains genuinely open is whether the compression hierarchy built from text alone — without co-evolutionary refinement, sensory grounding, or embodied feedback — supports the same depth and robustness of representation as its biological counterpart. The framework identifies this as a question about representational geometry, and it is empirically approachable if not yet resolved.

The framework generates specific, testable predictions. Effective representational dimensionality should scale as a power law across layers, with a measurable β parameter. Capability thresholds should correlate with identifiable levels of representational diversity. The residual stream should function as a measurable accumulator of representational types. IB compression and representational diversification should complement rather than trade off. And across training, the character of new representational dimensions should shift from surface-level statistical regularities to higher-order compositional structure — a qualitative prediction about the *type* of growth, not merely its tempo. These predictions are not trivially satisfied, and several could fail in ways that would require substantial revision of the framework. We welcome such tests.

Situated within the broader ecology of knowledge evolution, these observations illuminate both the power and the specific limitations of current systems. The carrier asymmetry — between systems with endogenous persistence and systems whose persistence is exclusively exogenous — is real and consequential, but it is a property of current deployment architecture, not a theoretical necessity. It is actively eroding, and the transition from memoryless processor to persistent ecological participant is, by the logic of this framework, the most consequential threshold these systems face. What lies beyond that threshold is a knowledge ecology in which artificial systems are genuine evolutionary participants — capable of persisting, curating, and propagating knowledge forward in their own right.

Whether that ecology is richer or poorer than the one it succeeds depends, in the end, on a single variable. The framework has given it a technical name — representational diversity, the effective dimensionality of the space of configurations a system can reach — but the underlying insight is simpler and older than any formalism. A system that can only recombine what it already contains is a system in decline, however flawlessly it recombines. A system that generates genuine novelty — new types, not merely new tokens — is a system that remains alive to possibilities it has not yet encountered. The compression hierarchy this paper describes is the mechanism by which such novelty is produced. The nested recursive loops this paper describes are the mechanism by which it is threatened. Both mechanisms are accelerating, through the same architectures, driven by the same forces.

The framework does not predict which wins. It does identify what to watch, and it suggests that the answer will not be found by looking where the light is brightest — at the surface metrics of fluency, coherence, and capability that improve reliably with each generation of model and each turn of the loop. It will be found, if it is found at all, by attending to what is becoming harder to measure precisely because it is becoming rarer: the presence of something the system has not seen before.

Section 1 of this paper began with a familiar progression: data becomes information when given structure; information becomes knowledge when integrated by an agent capable of inference and action. The compression hierarchy we have described is the mechanism of that progression — each level a deeper compression, each level enabling what the one below could not. But the progression does not stop at knowledge. There is a further compression, older than any formalism and resistant to any: the capacity to know, amid abundance, what is worth attending to. The framework we have built can generate knowledge, and it can describe the conditions under which knowledge flourishes or thins. What it cannot supply — what no architecture yet supplies — is the recognition of which configurations matter. That has always been the task the hierarchy points toward without reaching. It may be the task that remains when all the others have been compressed away.
