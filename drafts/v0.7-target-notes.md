# Validating DiSisto's compression theory of LLMs against current literature

**The paper's six core claims range from well-anticipated to genuinely novel, with the strongest originality lying in cross-domain synthesis rather than any single theoretical move.** Three claims face significant priority risk from existing work (rate-distortion layers, surprisal regimes, alignment-as-bandwidth), while three offer more distinctive contributions (biological bandwidth analogy, prediction-error broadcasts, grounding-as-rate-distortion). The overall framework is intellectually coherent but faces recurring methodological challenges around operationalization and the gap between conceptual metaphor and testable theory.

What follows is a claim-by-claim assessment drawing on literature from 2019–2026, covering information theory, computational mechanics, predictive processing, AI alignment, and linguistics.

## Claim 2: Three surprisal regimes and the "enrichment fraction" — the taxonomy is known, the metric may be new

The three-regime characterization — repetitive collapse at low surprisal, coherent generation at intermediate surprisal, noise at high surprisal — is **well-established in existing literature**, posing the highest priority risk of any claim in the paper.

**Basu et al.'s Mirostat (ICLR 2021)** is the most direct precursor. They identified a "boredom trap" (low perplexity → repetitive loops), a "confusion trap" (high perplexity → incoherent text), and an optimal regime in between, then built a feedback-control algorithm using perplexity as the diagnostic variable. This is functionally isomorphic to DiSisto's framework. Mikhaylovskiy et al. (2025) independently classified LLM text into three states — "solid" (periodic/repetitive), "critical" (power-law correlations), and "gas" (amorphous/random) — using autocorrelation analysis. Nakaishi et al. (2024) demonstrated a phase transition in GPT-2 at temperature ~1 between repetitive and incoherent regimes, with critical behavior at the boundary. Holtzman et al. (2020) implicitly described the same three regimes, and Meister et al. (2023) provided the information-theoretic explanation via locally typical sampling.

The use of **surprisal dynamics** (token-level trajectories rather than aggregate perplexity) for diagnostics has more recent but growing precedent. DivEye (Basani & Chen, 2025) showed second-order surprisal features contribute most to AI text detection. SurpMark (2025) demonstrated that surprisal state-transition patterns distinguish human from machine text even when marginal distributions converge. Alabdulmohsin & Steiner (2025) found the Hurst exponent of bits-per-token sequences (measuring fractal structure) correlates with text quality better than log-perplexity.

The term **"enrichment fraction" appears genuinely novel** — no use was found in NLP, machine learning, or information theory contexts. The concept bears resemblance to Mirostat's target surprise value τ and DivEye's surprisal-derived features, but the specific operationalization as a fraction appears original.

**Priority risk: HIGH for the regime taxonomy, LOW for the specific metric.** DiSisto must extensively cite Basu et al. (2021), Mikhaylovskiy et al. (2025), and Nakaishi et al. (2024). The novelty claim should rest on the "enrichment fraction" metric and any unique empirical demonstrations, not the three-regime observation itself.

## Claim 6: Grounding as rate-distortion — the most novel reframing, with a circularity risk

This appears to be the paper's **most original claim**, with no prior work explicitly reframing the symbol grounding problem as a rate-distortion question. The closest work comes from two directions. First, Zaslavsky, Kemp, Regier & Tishby's efficient communication program (PNAS 2018+) demonstrated that semantic systems across languages — color terms, kinship terms, numeral systems, container names — achieve near-optimal solutions to information bottleneck tradeoffs. This establishes that rate-distortion theory successfully describes how meaning is structured, providing the theoretical machinery DiSisto's claim requires. Second, Liu (2025) reformulated the grounding problem within algorithmic information theory, defining grounding as the ability to provide a compressed description — but this uses Kolmogorov complexity (lossless, binary) rather than rate-distortion theory (lossy, continuous).

The reframing directly challenges Bender & Koller's (2020) **binary form/meaning distinction**. Under a rate-distortion view, the question shifts from "does the system have access to meaning?" to "how much distortion-relevant structure does it preserve?" This aligns with a growing body of work arguing grounding is a matter of degree: Pavlick (2023) argued the debate should focus on characterizing representations rather than binary attributions; Mollo & Millière (2023) argued LLMs can achieve referential grounding through teleosemantics; Piantadosi & Hill (2022) proposed LLMs acquire meaning through functional roles. A very recent paper by Caglar et al. (March 2026) introduced "rate-distortion signatures" comparing human and machine vision, finding both follow a common lossy-compression principle but occupy different regions of RD space — directly supporting DiSisto's approach.

The claim's central vulnerability is the **distortion measure selection problem**: the entire framework depends on specifying what structure is "distortion-relevant," but this is precisely what is at issue in the grounding debate. For color naming, Zaslavsky et al. could use perceptual similarity in CIELAB space as an empirically grounded distortion measure. For general grounding, no such measure exists. A system could achieve low distortion under statistical co-occurrence patterns while remaining ungrounded under physical interaction affordances. This risks making the reframing **descriptively accurate but explanatorily circular** — the distortion measure encodes what counts as grounded, so saying "grounding is preserving distortion-relevant structure" reduces to "grounding is grounding the right things."

Xu et al. (2025, *Nature Human Behaviour*) found LLMs without sensory grounding recover non-sensorimotor but not sensorimotor features of human concepts, suggesting that for certain domains, distortion-relevant structure may be **fundamentally inaccessible from text alone**. This is compatible with the rate-distortion framing but sets hard limits on what text-only systems can achieve.

**Priority risk: LOW** — this specific reframing appears original. **Novelty: MODERATE-HIGH.** The synthesis connecting Zaslavsky's semantic efficiency program with the LLM grounding debate via rate-distortion theory is genuinely new. **Validity depends on operationalization** — without concrete instantiations of the distortion measure for specific grounding tasks, the framework risks remaining at the level of productive metaphor.

## Conclusion: a framework of novel bridges between well-mapped territories

DiSisto's paper attempts something ambitious: unifying transformer computation, generation dynamics, biological communication, alignment, and grounding under a single information-theoretic framework centered on compression, distortion, and bandwidth. The literature review reveals a consistent pattern across all six claims — **the individual components are well-established, but the specific cross-domain bridges are largely original**.

The paper's three greatest strengths are the grounding-as-rate-distortion reframing (Claim 6), the prediction-error broadcast interpretation of involuntary signals (Claim 4), and the biological bandwidth analogy (Claim 3). Its greatest vulnerability is the **three-regime surprisal taxonomy** (Claim 2), which is thoroughly anticipated by Mirostat, phase-transition analyses, and the decoding literature — this section needs extensive citation and repositioning. The rate-distortion layer claim (Claim 1) faces moderate-high priority risk from Tishby and Voita, and its most novel element (statistical complexity growing across depth) is currently untestable.

A recurring methodological concern spans the entire paper: the gap between information-theoretic metaphor and operationalizable theory. Claims about statistical complexity in transformer representations, distortion measures becoming "progressively more abstract," and rate-distortion characterizations of grounding all face the same challenge — they are mathematically suggestive but lack concrete procedures for measurement or falsification. The paper would benefit from identifying at least one claim where the framework generates a **specific, testable prediction** that differs from what existing theories would predict. Without this, the framework risks being appreciated for its elegance while remaining empirically inert.

You're right on all three, and these are worth working through carefully because they change the risk profile.

**On residual connections and the DPI:** Yes, the failure mode is in your favour. The strict DPI argument says information about input can't *increase* through a Markov chain of layers. Residual connections mean the system isn't a strict Markov chain — it's richer. So the actual information dynamics are *at least as interesting* as what the DPI-constrained model predicts, possibly more so. The framework's predictions about organizational complexity growing across depth become a conservative lower bound, not an overestimate. The genuine remaining concern is narrower: residual connections make it harder to *attribute* compression to individual layers cleanly, which complicates empirical validation. But that's a measurement problem, not a theoretical refutation.

**On layer pruning:** You're right, the claim doesn't require every layer to be doing critical rate-distortion work at capacity. Gromov finding redundant layers is compatible with the framework — it just means some layers are doing minimal compressive work, possibly re-encoding similar structure. The framework describes what the computation *is*, not that it's maximally efficient at every point. If anything, the pruning result raises an interesting question the framework could address: what distinguishes layers that are doing real compressive work from those that aren't? That's a prediction opportunity, not a refutation.

**On prediction error and involuntary signals:** Fair correction, and it matters a lot for defensibility. The weaker claim — that involuntary channels carry *significant information pertaining to* prediction error, among other things — is well-supported by existing evidence without requiring the strong interpretation that they're dedicated prediction-error channels. Pupil dilation tracking surprise, prosodic shifts at unexpected content, postural changes at conversational breakdowns — all of these are consistent with "includes significant prediction-error-relevant information" without needing to claim that's *all* they carry. The Fridlund objection (facial expressions as strategic tools) doesn't land against this version of the claim because you're not asserting exclusivity.

This reframing actually makes the overall argument structure tighter. The key point isn't that involuntary channels are *prediction error channels* — it's that they carry information about internal model states that the sender can't fully control, and prediction error is a significant component of that. The contrast with LLM interaction still holds: there's no analogous channel in either direction.

The **three-regime taxonomy** (Claim 2) still needs heavy citation of Mirostat — that's not a failure-mode-in-your-favour situation, that's a genuine priority issue where someone published a functionally equivalent observation with a working algorithm.
