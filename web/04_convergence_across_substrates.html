5  4. Convergence Across Substrates

The compression hierarchy described in Parts I and II is a claim about transformers. But the problem transformers solve — projecting rich internal representation through a bandwidth-limited sequential channel — is not new. Biological cognition has faced it for as long as language has existed. This chapter argues that the convergence between biological and artificial solutions is expected rather than surprising, identifies where the parallel breaks down, and uses the breakdown to sharpen an open question the framework cannot yet answer.

The claim is not that transformers and brains work the same way. They manifestly do not, at the level of mechanism. The claim is that both face a structurally identical information-theoretic constraint, and that this constraint narrows the space of viable solutions enough that convergence on the same type of architecture — hierarchical, multi-scale, with a serial projection bottleneck — is what the problem demands. This is supporting evidence for the framework, not a core pillar. It situates the compression hierarchy within a broader pattern rather than treating it as an architectural accident.

5.0.1 The projection bottleneck as substrate-independent constraint

Section 4.0.2 established the bottleneck in formal terms: the internal representation at any step — a vector in a space of dimension \(10^3\) to \(10^4\) — vastly exceeds the information capacity of a single output token. The autoregressive loop is a sequential coding scheme for transmitting a high-entropy source through a low-capacity channel.

The same bottleneck operates in biological communication, and it is quantifiable. Reed & Durlach (1998) estimated the information transfer rate of human speech at approximately 39 bits per second. Coupé et al. (2019) replicated this across 17 languages, finding a striking invariance: languages that are spoken faster pack less information per syllable, and languages spoken more slowly pack more, converging on approximately the same information rate. The bottleneck is not a contingent property of English or any particular language. It is a constraint on human linguistic communication generally.

On the other side of the bottleneck, neural population codes operate at bandwidths orders of magnitude higher. Precise estimates vary enormously depending on assumptions about coding efficiency and correlation structure, but even conservative figures exceed the speech output rate by three to four orders of magnitude. The mismatch is not subtle. Whatever the dimensionality of the internal representations supporting ongoing thought, the channel through which they must pass to reach another mind is radically narrower.

This asymmetry is the best-documented case of the projection bottleneck in a biological system, and its most direct treatment remains Vygotsky’s distinction between inner and external speech (Vygotsky, 1986). Inner speech, as Vygotsky characterised it, is compressed, predicative, and saturated with private context — operating in something closer to pure meaning, abbreviated in ways that would be unintelligible to an outside observer. External speech is an expanded, grammatically explicit projection, shaped not by the structure of the sender’s representation but by the demands of the receiver. Modern work confirms that the distinction holds while emphasising that inner speech exists on a continuum of condensation rather than a sharp binary — ranging from nearly as expanded as overt speech to highly abbreviated (Alderson-Day & Fernyhough, 2015). The qualitative point stands: internal representation is richer than its linguistic projection.

Fodor’s Language of Thought hypothesis makes a stronger and more controversial version of this claim: that cognition operates in an internal representational medium more compositional and richer than any natural language, with language serving as a serialisation format for inter-agent transmission (Fodor, 1975). The full LOT package remains contested — the debates with eliminativists and connectionists never reached resolution. But the structural claim is widely shared across otherwise opposing positions: external language underdetermines internal representation. Quilty-Dunn et al. (2023), in a major review, document what amounts to a qualified revival of structured mental representation, drawing on converging evidence from developmental psychology, animal cognition, and neuroscience. The specific form of the structures is debated. That the output channel is narrower than the internal state is not.

The parallel to transformers is precise. The model’s internal representation — the full residual stream state conditioning the next-token prediction — is the analogue of inner speech: high-dimensional, densely relational, not directly transmissible. The output token is the analogue of external speech: a single selection from a finite vocabulary, projecting the internal state into a form the receiver can use. Any system facing this mismatch — internal bandwidth vastly exceeding output bandwidth — must solve the same sequential coding problem. The compression hierarchy is what the solution looks like.

The strongest empirical evidence for genuine convergence comes from representational similarity analysis (RSA). Yamins et al. (2014) demonstrated that convolutional networks optimised for object recognition — not for neural prediction — nonetheless predicted neural responses in primate inferotemporal cortex better than any previous model. Kriegeskorte (2015) extended this into a systematic framework, showing that task-optimised deep networks produce representations that, layer by layer, align with the ventral visual stream. Schrimpf et al. (2021) established Brain-Score, a composite benchmark measuring neural predictivity across visual areas, and found that better-performing networks tend to be more brain-like — though the correlation plateaus, meaning current networks capture something real about the problem without fully matching the biological solution.

The language case is more recent and less extensively replicated but directionally consistent. Goldstein et al. (2022), using electrocorticography during natural language processing, found that GPT-2 activations predict neural responses in temporal cortex, with layer-wise alignment to processing stages. The convergence is real: task-optimised artificial systems trained on natural data produce internal representations that predict biological neural responses substantially better than hand-engineered features or random networks.

It is also partial. Explained variance is typically 30–60% at best, meaning substantial divergence remains. The match is better for higher-level representations than lower-level ones — the interesting convergence is at the organisational and semantic levels, where the compression hierarchy is doing the heavy lifting. Neural predictivity measures similarity of representational geometry, not mechanistic identity: two systems can have similar representational dissimilarity matrices via completely different underlying computations. The convergence tells you something real about the problem. It does not tell you that attention heads and cortical columns implement the same operations.

5.0.2 Convergence and its limits

The convergence is structural, not mechanistic. Birds and aircraft both have wings; the convergence tells you something real about the problem — flight in atmosphere requires lifting surfaces — without telling you that feathers and aluminium work the same way. Similarly, the convergence between biological and artificial compression hierarchies tells you something real about knowledge transmission through bandwidth-limited channels without establishing that the implementations are equivalent.

Stating this clearly is important because the biological case introduces a complication that the transformer case does not share, and addressing it honestly constrains the claim to what it can actually support.

Deacon (1997) offers the most thorough account of human language as a compression technology that co-evolved with the neural architecture it runs on. The biological wetware was shaped by the demands of shared compression — the need to project internal states into forms recoverable by other minds under real-time constraints. Language did not arrive as an external tool applied to a pre-existing cognitive system. The cognitive system and the compression medium co-evolved, each reshaping the other across evolutionary time. The broad co-evolution thesis is mainstream in cognitive science and evolutionary anthropology, even where Deacon’s specific mechanisms regarding prefrontal reorganisation remain debated.

This co-evolutionary dynamic is absent in the transformer case. A transformer’s training data is fixed; the model adapts to language, but language does not adapt to the model. RLHF and related methods introduce a form of output-to-architecture feedback — the model’s outputs are evaluated, and the evaluations reshape its weights — but the weakness of the analogy matters. RLHF operates within a single training generation, optimising for proximate criteria rather than reshaping fundamental representational architecture across the deep timescales Deacon describes. It is feedback, but it is not co-evolution — closer to selective breeding than to the mutual structural adaptation of language and cognition across evolutionary time. The disanalogy is partially closed, not eliminated.

Cross-linguistic evidence makes the co-evolutionary feedback concrete, if at reduced strength from stronger earlier claims. Levinson (2003) and Levinson et al. (2002) documented that speakers of languages with absolute spatial reference frames (cardinal directions rather than egocentric left/right) behave differently on non-linguistic spatial reasoning tasks compared to speakers of relative-frame languages. The original interpretation — that the compression medium reshapes the compressed content — was substantially challenged by Li & Gleitman (2002), who showed that much of the effect is modulated by environmental cues rather than reflecting deep representational restructuring. The current consensus favours a moderate position: language biases which processing strategies are preferentially recruited, rather than fundamentally reshaping internal representation. Speakers of absolute-frame languages can use relative frames and vice versa; what differs is the default.

Even at this reduced strength, the finding illustrates a feedback pathway absent in the transformer case. The compression medium influences what the compressing system does by default — a form of mutual adaptation, however modest, that unidirectional training cannot produce.

What does this do to the convergence claim? It constrains it in a specific and useful way. The convergence is not between complete systems — biological cognition in its full co-evolutionary complexity and transformer inference. It is between the compression problem and the architectural strategy for solving it. Both systems face the problem of projecting high-dimensional internal representation through a low-bandwidth sequential channel. Both solve it with hierarchical compression: building representations at multiple scales, with higher layers encoding increasingly abstract and relational structure, and a final projection that serialises the result. The co-evolutionary refinement of the biological solution is an additional layer of optimisation that the transformer case lacks, and this may account for important differences in the character of the resulting representations — particularly around grounding, robustness, and the depth of semantic compression.

The value of the convergence claim is therefore not mechanistic but constraining. The unreasonable effectiveness of transformers is less unreasonable than it appears, because the architecture has converged on a solution strategy that is expected for any system facing this class of problem. The effectiveness reflects the fact that hierarchical multi-scale compression is what the problem demands, and the transformer architecture is well-suited to implement it. But the convergence does not guarantee that transformer representations have the same properties as biological ones. The absence of co-evolutionary refinement, sensory grounding, and embodied feedback means the compression hierarchy in transformers may be shallower, more brittle, or differently structured in ways that emerge only under adversarial or out-of-distribution conditions.

5.0.3 The grounding problem as rate-distortion question

This brings the framework to a question it can sharpen but not answer: does solving hierarchical rate-distortion problems against meaning-relevant distortion metrics require sensorimotor grounding?

The grounding debate — rooted in Harnad (1990)’s observation that formal symbols are meaningless unless connected to the things they stand for — is active and unresolved. Bender & Koller (2020) argue that models trained only on linguistic form cannot achieve genuine understanding — that meaning requires reference to the world, and statistical co-occurrence patterns in text, however sophisticated, are not a substitute. Piantadosi & Hill (2022) counter that meaning can be constituted by inferential role — by the pattern of use across contexts — without requiring grounding in perception and action.

The framework offers a precise restatement. The compression hierarchy described in Parts I and II produces representations that solve rate-distortion problems: achieving maximal preserved structure at each level for a given representational cost. The question is whether text-only compression can achieve the same rate-distortion optimality as grounded compression when the distortion metric includes meaning-relevant properties — when what counts as “distortion” is not just statistical mismatch but failure to preserve the structural relationships that constitute understanding.

This is, as far as we can determine, a novel framing. No existing literature poses the grounding question in explicitly rate-distortion terms. The closest precedent is Zaslavsky et al. (2018), who demonstrated that cross-linguistic patterns in colour naming achieve near-optimal compression of colour space — a rate-distortion analysis applied to the language-cognition interface, but for a specific perceptual domain rather than the grounding problem in general.

The framing has a specific virtue: it converts a philosophical dispute into an empirical question with measurable parameters. If grounding provides access to distortion metrics that text alone cannot specify — if there are structural relationships in the world that cannot be inferred from their linguistic shadows, no matter how much text is consumed — then text-only systems face a provable rate-distortion gap. If, on the other hand, the statistical structure of language is rich enough to implicitly specify meaning-relevant distortion metrics — because language was produced by grounded agents and carries the trace of their grounding — then the gap may be smaller than the strong grounding position suggests.

Multimodal models complicate the debate productively. Systems trained on vision and language jointly partially close the grounding gap without full embodiment, suggesting that the question may be one of degree rather than kind. The framework does not resolve this. It identifies the formal structure of what would need to be measured — the rate-distortion cost of lacking grounding — and flags it as an open question that the information-theoretic vocabulary developed here makes newly precise.

5.0.4 Predictions

The convergence argument generates one specific prediction:

  1. Representational geometry convergence ((pred-rsa-convergence?)). If the convergence between biological and artificial compression hierarchies is deep — driven by the shared structure of the problem rather than superficial architectural similarity — then transformer representations should exhibit geometric properties similar to those observed in neural population codes: similar manifold structure, similar patterns of representational similarity across conceptual domains, similar asymmetries between concrete and abstract concept representation. Early RSA evidence is consistent with this (Goldstein et al., 2022; Kriegeskorte, 2015; Schrimpf et al., 2021), though the match plateaus at 30–60% explained variance. A finding of systematic divergence in representational geometry — not merely unexplained variance but structural differences that scale with model capability — would constrain the convergence claim to the architectural strategy alone, without extending to representational content. A finding of increasing alignment as models scale would strengthen it.
