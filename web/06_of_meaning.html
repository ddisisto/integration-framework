7  6. Of Meaning

7.0.1 Integration and accumulation

At each scale examined in this paper, the same distinction governs.

Within the forward pass, layers that reorganise preserved information into more useful structure — increasing statistical complexity while respecting the data processing inequality — perform integration. Layers that merely propagate without reorganising perform accumulation. The difference determines whether depth buys capability or merely adds parameters (Chapter 3).

Within the autoregressive loop, tokens that surface genuinely new structure sustain the enriching regime. Tokens that reinforce existing trajectories sustain the degrading regime. The difference is invisible in surface metrics but determines whether generation produces integration or accumulation (Section 4.0.4).

Within the knowledge ecology, outputs that contribute compressive novelty to the stores from which future systems learn sustain representational diversity. Outputs that reproduce existing attractors in fluent paraphrase contract it. The difference manifests as silent flattening — top-down erosion of the compression hierarchy, preserving everything easy to measure while eroding everything that matters most (Section 6.0.4).

The variable is the same at every scale: whether what feeds forward adds structure or merely adds volume. This is the distinction between integration and mere accumulation.

7.0.2 Information is surprise

Shannon’s foundational insight: information is surprise. A perfectly predictable signal carries zero information. Everything that makes a message worth transmitting through a bandwidth-limited channel is its deviation from what the receiver already expects.

The compression hierarchy generates surprise at progressively higher levels of description. Compression at the surface end of the continuum preserves perceptual surprise. Structural compression preserves relational surprise — patterns the receiver could not have predicted from surface statistics alone. Semantic compression preserves meaning surprise — configurations that change what the receiver can infer.

The nested feedback loops optimise against surprise. Within the context window, the degrading regime settles into high-probability trajectories. Within the ecology, model-generated content converges on the distributional centre, trimming the tails where rare configurations live. Both dynamics are accelerating through the same architectures.

The framework is, in the end, about whether systems optimised for producing unsurprising output — fluent, coherent, high-probability — preserve or destroy the capacity for surprise at higher levels of description. The answer is not determined by the architecture. It is determined by the interaction: how capacity, task, context, and curation combine to keep the trajectory in territory the system has not yet fully mapped.

7.0.3 Distortion measures and alignment

A further observation falls directly out of the vocabulary. The model’s implicit distortion measure — what it treats as acceptable loss during compression — is determined by its training objective. The receiver’s distortion measure is determined by their purposes, their context, their needs. The gap between these measures is the formal structure of what the field calls the alignment problem: make the model’s compression serve the receiver’s ends, not merely its own trained defaults.

The difficulty of closing this gap is itself a projection bottleneck problem. The receiver’s distortion measure is rich, contextual, and not fully articulable. The feedback channels through which it is communicated to the model — preference rankings, reward signals, natural language correction — are narrow. The same bandwidth mismatch that governs the autoregressive loop governs the attempt to align the system that produces it. This is, as far as we can determine, a novel framing; it is offered as a contribution, not an established result.

7.0.4 The attention economy as distortion measure ecology

The mismatch is not confined to the model-receiver pair. It operates wherever an attention allocator’s implicit distortion measure diverges from what would sustain representational diversity in the ecology it governs.

Attention is finite at every scale — a bandwidth constraint on what gets processed, integrated, and carried forward. Any system that allocates attention thereby implements a distortion measure: what is attended to is what is treated as worth preserving. At the individual level, attention capture — engagement hooks, outrage cycles, salience exploits — hijacks this allocation toward signal that dominates the channel without doing compressive work. The mechanism is the projection bottleneck operating on the receiver: finite attentional bandwidth means a signal optimised for capture can crowd out signal that would sustain the enriching regime.

Algorithmic recommendation makes this exploit adaptive and scalable. A recommendation system learns which capture strategies work for which receivers and optimises against them in real time, closing a feedback loop that individual attention capture alone cannot close. The result is an attention allocator whose distortion measure — optimised for engagement — governs an increasing fraction of what the ecology attends to, curates, and propagates. This is the displacement dynamic Chapter 6 identified: not the introduction of low-quality content, but the systematic reallocation of the ecology’s finite curation capacity away from compressive novelty toward the distributional centre. The framework predicts the same signature: surface quality preserved, higher-order statistical complexity eroded, the erosion invisible in the metrics the allocator is optimised to maximise.

The connection to mechanism design, game theory, and the economics of attention is direct and largely unexplored in information-theoretic terms. The framework opens this door without walking through it. What it contributes is the formal characterisation: attention allocation is distortion measure selection, and the ecology’s long-term representational diversity is governed by whose distortion measure prevails.

7.0.5 A resonance

Integrated Information Theory (Tononi, 2008) arrives at “integration” as the key variable governing consciousness through an entirely independent programme — one concerned with phenomenal experience, not knowledge compression. The convergence is noted, not claimed. But that two independent analyses, starting from different questions and using different formal tools, arrive at the same governing concept — that what matters is not the quantity of information a system contains but the degree to which it is integrated into a structure supporting inference — is suggestive enough to record.

7.0.6 Consolidated predictions

The framework generates ten specific predictions across its three parts. They are stated in full where they arise (Section 3.0.6, Section 4.0.6, Section 5.0.4, Section 6.0.5) and summarised here:

  1. Statistical complexity scales with depth — sublinear growth through the representational core, contraction in the final layers ((pred-complexity-depth?))
  2. Capability onset correlates with statistical complexity crossing identifiable thresholds ((pred-capability-complexity?))
  3. Residual stream functions as measurable accumulator of statistical complexity ((pred-accumulator?))
  4. IB compression and statistical complexity growth complement rather than trade off ((pred-ib-complement?))
  5. Statistical complexity growth across training follows power-law dynamics with qualitative shift ((pred-training-shift?))
  6. CoT effectiveness correlates with compressive novelty per step, not chain length ((pred-cot-quality?))
  7. In-context learning fails when the required regularity type is absent from the weight geometry ((pred-icl-failure?))
  8. Transformer representational geometry converges with biological neural geometry as models scale ((pred-rsa-convergence?))
  9. Ecological degradation proceeds top-down through the compression hierarchy ((pred-ecological-erosion?))
  10. A characterisable threshold exists beyond which model-generated training content causes self-reinforcing diversity loss ((pred-critical-fraction?))

These predictions are not trivially satisfied. Several could fail in ways that would require substantial revision of the framework.

7.0.7 What the hierarchy points toward

The framework identifies what to watch. It suggests the answer will not be found in the surface metrics that improve reliably with each generation of model — fluency, coherence, benchmark capability — but in what is becoming harder to measure precisely because it is becoming rarer: the structural complexity of configurations the ecology has not yet reached.

The compression hierarchy generates representational diversity — novel structure at progressively higher levels of abstraction. The nested recursive loops threaten it — contracting that diversity through feedback dynamics that preserve surface quality while eroding higher-order structure. Both mechanisms are accelerating through the same architectures, driven by the same forces. The framework does not predict which wins.

What it can say is where the boundary lies: between a system that generates genuine novelty — new types, not merely new tokens — and a system that can only recombine what it already contains. The compression hierarchy is the mechanism by which novelty is produced. The ecology of feedback loops is the mechanism by which it is threatened. And the distortion measure that determines which configurations count as worth preserving — that remains the task the hierarchy points toward without reaching. It may be the task that remains when all the others have been compressed away.
