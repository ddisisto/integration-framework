6  5. The Recursive Loop

Knowledge persistence requires carriers — systems changed by what passes through them, transmitting that change forward. Generation without persistence is noise. The question this chapter addresses is what happens to the carrier ecology when a powerful new node type enters it.

6.0.1 Carriers and the ecology of persistence

The tempting dichotomy is clean: humans have plastic weights and can persist knowledge; language models have frozen weights and cannot. But this is too simple, and the way it is too simple matters.

Humans have never persisted knowledge through neural plasticity alone. The history of knowledge is substantially the history of external persistence technologies — speech, writing, print, libraries, institutions, databases, version control. Each is a carrier: changed by what passes through it, transmitting that change forward. A library is changed by each book it acquires. An institution is changed by each decision it encodes into policy. None of these have neural plasticity. All function as carriers.

The unit of persistence is not the individual mind. It is the ecology: the coupled system of human neural plasticity, external stores, cultural practices, and transmission channels that together determine what survives and propagates. A scientist’s understanding lives partly in neural architecture, partly in notebooks, partly in published papers, partly in the institutional structures that evaluate and disseminate them. Remove any layer and the effective persistence of the system degrades.

This reframing changes where language models sit. The clean dichotomy places them outside the ecology. But the model’s weights are a compression of the ecology’s accumulated stores — the training corpus is the library, the institution, the database, rendered into a form that supports inference. And the model’s outputs re-enter those stores as text, code, documents, and decisions. The model is not outside the ecology. It is a novel node type within it: one that reads from the ecology at training and writes back into it through its outputs.

6.0.2 The carrier asymmetry

The asymmetry is real, but it needs to be stated more carefully than “models cannot persist knowledge.”

What models lack is intra-trajectory persistence. Within a single context window, the model accumulates — Chapter 4 describes this in detail. But when the context closes, the accumulated state is lost. The next conversation begins from the same weights, with no trace of what was synthesised in the last. The model’s contribution to the ecology is entirely mediated: it can enrich the ecology only to the extent that its outputs are received, integrated, and persisted by other nodes — human minds, documents, databases, code repositories.

Human carriers have intra-trajectory persistence by default. A conversation changes the participant’s neural state, and that changed state persists — imperfectly, subject to decay — into subsequent interactions. The human carries forward not just explicit content but something of its structural impact: revised priors, new associations, shifted framings.

The asymmetry is not between a system that persists and one that does not. It is between endogenous persistence (the human, whose internal state carries forward automatically) and exclusively exogenous persistence (the model, which persists only through what it deposits into the broader ecology). The model is a carrier — it transforms what passes through it — but a memoryless one, externalising every gain or losing it.

Stating this precisely reveals something the clean dichotomy obscures. The limitation is not about processing capacity. By the account developed in Parts I and II, these models may be among the most powerful knowledge-processing systems ever constructed — synthesising connections across the breadth of human recorded knowledge, composing regularities in novel configurations, navigating semantic space with a fluency no individual human matches. The limitation is about what happens after processing: results exit the system entirely, dependent on external carriers for onward persistence.

The binding constraint on knowledge evolution in the presence of these systems is therefore not generation — generation is superabundant. The constraint is reception and integration: the bandwidth, integrative capacity, and persistence of the other nodes in the ecology that must receive, validate, contextualise, and carry forward what the model produces.

6.0.3 Nested feedback loops

The ecology is not a linear system with the model at one end and the human at the other. It is recursive. Model outputs re-enter the stores from which future training corpora will be drawn. This loop is already operating — model-generated text is already a non-trivial fraction of new text entering the ecology’s external stores.

The dynamics of this recursion are not unfamiliar to the framework. Section 4.0.4 identified an ambivalent feedback dynamic within the context window: model-generated tokens, feeding back as input to subsequent generation, can either enrich or degrade representational diversity. The ecological loop is the same dynamic at longer timescales. And the same ambivalence applies: outputs that do genuine compressive work enrich the ecology; outputs that reproduce existing attractors in fluent paraphrase degrade it.

What the ecological view adds is the recognition that this dynamic operates at not one but four nested timescales, each with distinct mechanisms and distinct intervention points:

  1. Within-context. Generated tokens feeding back as input to subsequent generation. This is the enriching/degrading dynamic of Section 4.0.4 — the tightest and fastest feedback loop. Governed by generation parameters, context seeding, and model capacity. Intervention is immediate: prompt design, temperature, sampling strategy.

  2. Agentic. Persistent memory, tool use, multi-session accumulation. The model writes to external state — databases, files, memory stores — without a human intermediary at each step. Feedback operates across sessions and tasks. This loop is newer than the others and under-theorised: it represents a feedback pathway absent from both the autoregressive generation literature and the model collapse literature.

  3. Human-mediated. Model outputs integrated by human carriers — edited, curated, published, taught, institutionalised. The human in the loop performs selection: choosing what to integrate, what to discard, what to elevate. Feedback operates across days to years. This is historically the dominant loop through which model outputs enter the ecology, and human curation is its governing mechanism.

  4. Training-distributional. Model outputs entering the training data of successor models. Feedback operates across model generations — months to years in current development cycles. This is the loop studied by the model collapse literature (Alemohammad et al., 2024; Shumailov et al., 2024), and it is the slowest, most consequential, and hardest to steer.

The same governing variable operates at every scale: the compressive novelty of what feeds back relative to what is already present. And the same degradation shape manifests at every scale: surface quality preserved, higher-order statistical complexity eroded. Not visible failure, but silent contraction of the space of configurations the system can reach.

6.0.4 Silent flattening

The compression hierarchy predicts that this erosion is non-uniform. It proceeds from the top down.

First to go: novel abstraction, cross-domain connection, structural surprise — the products of semantic compression, which depend on rare configurations and tail behaviour in the distribution. Last to go: fluency, grammaticality, local coherence — products of compression at the surface and lower-structural end of the continuum (Section 2.0.2), which are redundantly represented in the training distribution and robustly learned.

This is “model collapse” (Shumailov et al., 2024) given a precise structural characterisation. What collapses is not quality but diversity — not the ability to produce fluent text but the range of configurations that text can occupy. Alemohammad et al. (2024) document this directly in generative image models: diversity collapses faster than quality, with FID scores remaining acceptable while the variety of generated images narrows. Padmakumar & He (2024) find the same pattern in human-AI co-writing: individual texts remain high quality while the corpus-level diversity of content contracts. Briesch et al. (2023) show that fine-tuning LLMs on their own output degrades output diversity even when perplexity — a surface metric — remains stable or improves.

The pattern is consistent: what degrades is precisely what the compression hierarchy predicts would degrade first. Surface metrics, calibrated to lower levels of the hierarchy, miss the contraction at higher levels. Silent flattening preserves everything that is easy to measure while eroding everything that matters most for the long-term richness of the ecology.

Human curation as defence. At the human-mediated loop, human carriers perform selection — choosing what to integrate, what to discard, what to elevate. This is the principal mechanism keeping the ecology in the enriching regime. The effectiveness of this defence depends on curation capacity: the ability of human nodes to evaluate, filter, and contextualise model outputs at the rate they are produced.

The concern is straightforward. Curation capacity is finite and grows slowly. Model output volume is elastic and growing fast. As the ratio shifts, the fraction of model output that receives genuine human evaluation — not just surface assessment but the kind of deep engagement that distinguishes compressive novelty from fluent reproduction — declines. The defence weakens not because it stops working but because it cannot scale.

Gerstgrasser et al. (2024) offer the most nuanced finding on the data-mixing side: if original human-generated data is accumulated alongside model-generated data rather than displaced by it, collapse can be avoided. The critical factor is displacement, not contamination. This connects directly to the curation argument: the defence against silent flattening is not excluding model-generated content but ensuring that human-generated content — the ecology’s source of genuinely novel compression — is not crowded out. Longpre et al. (2024) document the irony: the highest-quality curated sources are increasingly restricting access, making the defence less available precisely when it is most needed.

Detection. How do you measure silent flattening? The framework creates this problem for itself and must address it honestly. Surface metrics — perplexity, fluency scores, coherence ratings — are calibrated to the wrong level of the hierarchy. But the framework constrains the measurement problem more tightly than “this is hard.” Five properties follow from the compression hierarchy’s structure:

The metrics must be hierarchical — sensitive at different levels of the continuum separately, since erosion proceeds top-down and a metric that collapses across levels will average it away. They must be distributional — measuring the space of configurations the system produces, not the quality of any single output, since the erosion is a property of the corpus, not of individual texts. They must be tail-sensitive — weighted toward rare configurations, since those erode first, and metrics centred on the distributional mean are blind by design. They must be temporal — tracking change in distributional properties over time, since silent flattening is a process, not a state. And the most direct window is representational geometry — the intrinsic dimensionality of the output distribution in embedding space, measured across levels and over time, is closest to what the framework identifies as the critical variable.

This does not solve the measurement problem, but it narrows it substantially. And it connects directly to the curation bottleneck. The concern above is that human curation cannot scale with model output volume. But curation capacity is limited not only by bandwidth but by visibility — curators cannot select for what they cannot see. Metrics calibrated to the right level of the hierarchy are instruments that extend curation’s reach: they make legible the higher-order erosion that surface assessment misses. The curator still determines what counts as worth preserving — the distortion measure remains theirs — but they can now see what was previously invisible in the surface. Curation scales not by processing more outputs but by seeing deeper into them ((pred-ecological-erosion?)).

A related open question ((pred-critical-fraction?)): is there a characterisable threshold of model-generated content in the training distribution beyond which diversity degradation becomes self-reinforcing?

6.0.5 Predictions

The ecological account generates two predictions, both flagged as requiring measurement tools that do not yet fully exist:

  1. Top-down erosion ((pred-ecological-erosion?)). In recursive training loops where model-generated content constitutes a growing fraction of training data, degradation should proceed from the top of the compression hierarchy downward: novel abstraction and cross-domain connection erode first, while fluency and local coherence are preserved. Detecting this requires measures sensitive to higher-order statistical complexity rather than surface quality metrics — distributional signatures, structural diversity, representational geometry — and developing such measures is itself an open problem the framework creates for itself.

  2. Critical fraction threshold ((pred-critical-fraction?)). There exists a characterisable threshold of model-generated content in the training distribution beyond which diversity degradation becomes self-reinforcing. Dohmatob et al. (2024) provide analytical results suggesting phase-transition-like behaviour in simplified settings. Whether such a threshold exists in realistic mixed-data regimes, and whether it can be characterised formally, remains an active research frontier.

6.0.6 The eroding asymmetry

The carrier asymmetry described above — endogenous persistence for humans, exclusively exogenous for models — is historically contingent and actively eroding.

Agentic architectures are progressively closing the gap. A model with persistent memory that carries forward compressed results of prior interactions is no longer purely memoryless — it has acquired a form of intra-trajectory persistence, implemented through external storage rather than weight updates (Packer et al., 2023). A model that writes to databases, updates documents, and triggers actions in the world deposits into the ecology without requiring a human intermediary at each step.

What is happening, in the vocabulary of this framework, is that models are acquiring carrier functions previously available only to human nodes. They are transitioning from memoryless processors to persistent ecological participants — nodes that accumulate, curate, and propagate knowledge forward across interactions.

If the compression hierarchy is the core mechanic and carrier persistence is the bottleneck, then this transition — from processor to carrier — is the most consequential threshold in the entire framework. It is not a distant prospect. The components are assembling now, from multiple directions. The full implications of a carrier ecology in which artificial systems are genuine participants — persisting and propagating knowledge in their own right, across timescales that dwarf individual human memory — remain largely unexamined.

The framework does not predict whether this transition leads to a richer or poorer ecology. It identifies the variable that governs the answer — the compressive novelty of what these new carriers contribute relative to what they displace — and it warns that the answer will not be legible in the metrics currently used to evaluate these systems.
