Integration

Compression, composition, and the ecology of representational diversity in large language models

Author

Daniel

Published

March 19, 2026

Abstract

The effectiveness of large language models reflects hierarchical multi-scale compression — organisational and semantic, not merely algorithmic — operating simultaneously within the forward pass and sequentially across the autoregressive loop. The autoregressive loop is fundamentally ambivalent: it can enrich or degrade representational diversity through the same mechanism. This ambivalence recurs at the ecological scale, where model outputs re-enter the knowledge ecosystem through nested feedback loops, threatening a silent flattening that preserves surface quality while eroding higher-order structure.

Information theory provides the natural formal language: rate-distortion theory characterises the hierarchy, the data processing inequality clarifies what depth can and cannot do, channel capacity formalises the projection bottleneck, and statistical complexity provides the measure of what grows across depth. The framework generates specific testable predictions and identifies representational diversity — the structural complexity of the configurations a system can reach — as the critical variable governing whether these systems enrich or impoverish the knowledge ecology they are entering.

Part I — Compression

1 1. The Compression Hierarchy

A transformer trained on a large text corpus builds, across its layers, progressively more abstract representations of its input — from surface statistics through syntactic structure to semantic relationships. At each layer, the representation is compressed: information about the specific input is traded for structure that generalises across inputs. The final layers project this rich internal state through a radical bottleneck — a single token selected from a finite vocabulary — and the autoregressive loop repeats the process, constructing a sequential code that carries, one token at a time, what no single token could express.

This is compression, but not in the sense the word usually evokes. It is not reducing file sizes. It is building internal structure that preserves what matters — for progressively more abstract definitions of “matters” — while discarding what does not. Understanding what these systems do, what they are capable of, and where they fail requires taking this compression seriously and asking what kind of compression it is.

1.1 The one sharp boundary

There is exactly one clean distinction in the landscape of compression: lossless versus lossy.

Lossless compression — Huffman coding, Lempel-Ziv, arithmetic coding — exploits statistical redundancy to reduce bits without discarding anything. The original signal is perfectly recoverable. No judgement is required about what matters; everything is preserved. A gzip model of English text has learned nothing about English. It has learned which byte sequences recur in a particular file. Applied to a different file, it starts from scratch. This is compression in the narrowest and most common sense of the word: reduction without loss, pattern-matching without understanding.

The moment compression becomes lossy, something qualitatively different enters. A lossy compressor must decide what to discard — which means it must decide what counts as acceptable distortion. And that decision is not given by the data. It is a choice, made by or on behalf of an agent, reflecting what that agent considers worth preserving.

Rate-distortion theory (Shannon, 1959) formalises this precisely: the minimum rate (bits) required to encode a source at a given level of distortion is determined by the distortion measure — the function specifying what counts as loss. Shannon’s deepest insight for the present framework is that the distortion measure is a choice. It encodes what the compressor treats as relevant. This is the point at which perspective enters compression. Not subjectivity in the philosophical sense — JPEG’s psychoacoustic model is grounded in the physiology of human hearing — but relevance: compression relative to a receiver, a purpose, a context.

1.2 The compression continuum

Above the lossless boundary, compression is not a set of discrete levels but a continuum, defined by the abstraction at which the distortion measure operates. The distortion measure shifts — from surface fidelity to structural fidelity to meaning fidelity — and with each shift, the compressor discards more surface detail while preserving something more abstract and more useful.

At one end, lossy compression preserves perceptual fidelity. JPEG quantises high-frequency components that the human visual system cannot easily distinguish. MP3 uses psychoacoustic masking to discard sounds that fall below the threshold of audibility. The distortion measure is calibrated to a sensory system: what will the receiver not notice? Surface detail is lost, but the signal as perceived is preserved. These compressors have a model of their receiver. They do not have a model of their content.

Further along, compression preserves structural fidelity. A grammar compresses a language: a finite set of rules generates an unbounded set of valid sentences. A taxonomy compresses a domain: structural relationships from which properties of individual instances can be partly inferred. PCA compresses a dataset into its axes of maximum variance. The difference between memorising every chess game ever played and learning the principles of chess is the difference between surface compression and structural compression. The latter generalises to positions never seen, because it has captured the shape of the domain rather than the surface of the data. What counts as distortion here is not perceptual degradation but structural misrepresentation — a grammar that generates invalid sentences, a set of principles that leads to losing positions.

At the far end, compression preserves meaning. In a well-formed semantic representation, concepts that are related are geometrically proximate. Analogical relationships are preserved as structural regularities — vector arithmetic in embedding spaces capturing semantic relations (Mikolov et al., 2013) is an elementary example of what, at scale, becomes a rich geometry supporting inference through navigation. Surface form and even structural detail can be freely discarded; what must be preserved is the geometry of meaning itself. Distortion at this level is not signal degradation or structural error but meaning failure: representations that place unrelated concepts nearby, that destroy analogical structure, that support invalid inferences.

The boundaries between these regions are not sharp. PCA applied to sensor data discovers statistical structure; the same operation applied to word co-occurrence matrices discovers latent semantic dimensions. The mathematics does not change. What changes is the structure present in the input and the relevance criterion implicit in the task. JPEG’s perceptual model already embeds a relevance judgement that goes beyond surface fidelity. A grammar already encodes something about meaning through the structures it permits. The continuum is real, and the regions blur into each other.

What does not blur is the direction. Movement along this continuum is characterised by three consistent shifts: the distortion measure becomes more abstract, more surface detail is sacrificed, and the resulting representation supports richer downstream use. A system operating at the perceptual end can reproduce; at the structural end, it can generalise; at the semantic end, it can reason by navigation. Each level presupposes and extends the one below — semantic compression requires structural regularities to have been discovered before their meaning relationships can be encoded, and structural compression operates on input that has already been rendered into tractable form.

1.3 What transformers do

Return now to the transformer. Tishby & Zaslavsky (2015) provide the bridge: each layer of a deep network can be understood as solving a rate-distortion problem, compressing its input while preserving information relevant to the task. Achille & Soatto (2018) show that the resulting minimal sufficient statistics naturally produce invariant and disentangled representations — representations with structure that goes beyond what mutual information with the input captures.

Probing studies confirm the empirical picture: lower layers encode surface and lexical features, middle layers encode syntactic structure, upper layers encode semantic and relational structure (Jawahar et al., 2019; Tenney et al., 2019). Delétang et al. (2024) demonstrate the connection operationally: language models are literal compressors, achieving compression rates that scale with model capability — the better the model, the fewer bits per token, because it has captured more of the source’s structure. The compression continuum is not merely a conceptual framework. It is a description of what the forward pass does — traversing from surface toward meaning, with each layer solving a rate-distortion problem at a progressively more abstract level of relevance. Section 2 will make this precise.

The hierarchy is not substrate-specific. Any system that must compress for transmission through a bandwidth-limited channel — biological or artificial — faces this continuum. Section 4 will develop the claim with the biological evidence. For now, the point is that the continuum describes a property of the problem, not of any particular architecture. The transformer is one implementation. The question the framework addresses is what follows from taking hierarchical compression seriously as the central operation these systems perform.

2 2. Structure Across Depth

Section 1 established what compression becomes when the distortion measure shifts from surface fidelity toward meaning: a continuum along which progressively more abstract structure is preserved. This chapter is about how transformers implement that continuum — how the forward pass generates progressively deeper compression across layers, what formal framework captures what is happening, and what specific predictions follow.

2.1 How transformers generate representational structure

Within a transformer, each layer does not merely re-encode the input. It synthesises representational configurations — features, relationships, abstractions — that did not exist at the layer below. Three architectural features drive this.

Attention as variable-scale compression. Each attention head discovers structure at whatever scale the data demands — local syntax, long-range dependency, abstract analogy — and reweights accordingly. Multiple heads operating in parallel mean multiple simultaneous compression schemes on the same input, each solving a different rate-distortion problem at a different scale.

Residual streams as accumulation. Each layer adds to rather than replaces the internal representation. Earlier compressions are not discarded but elaborated. The architecture accumulates representational structure across depth rather than trading old for new. This is among the best-established findings in mechanistic interpretability: the residual stream functions as a shared communication channel across layers, with each layer reading from and writing to it (Elhage et al., 2021). The logit lens (nostalgebraist, 2020) and tuned lens (Belrose et al., 2023) provide direct empirical windows — applying the output projection at intermediate layers reveals progressively refined predictions, with each layer’s contribution measurable as an incremental update to the accumulated representation.

Depth as recursive compression. Each layer operates on the output of the last, applying compression to already-compressed representations. Each pass can surface structure invisible at the previous scale — regularities in the regularities.

2.2 The data processing inequality and what grows across depth

There is an apparent tension in the claim that layers generate new representational structure. The data processing inequality (DPI) states that processing cannot increase mutual information about the source: if \(X \to Y \to Z\) forms a Markov chain, then \(I(X; Z) \leq I(X; Y)\) (Cover & Thomas, 2006). Layers cannot create information about the input that was not already present.

The resolution is the core formal move of this chapter. What layers generate is not new information about the input but new organisation of existing information. Making implicit structure explicit. Surfacing regularities that were present but computationally inaccessible.

The DPI constrains total mutual information with the source. It says nothing about how that information is arranged in the representation. A layer that transforms a raw signal into a representation where previously latent structure is geometrically manifest — where distance in the representation space reflects meaningful similarity — has not violated the DPI. It has reorganised preserved information into a form that supports new kinds of inference.

Achille & Soatto (2018) provide the closest formal treatment: they show that minimal sufficient statistics for the task — the solution to a rate-distortion problem with relevance to the label — naturally produce invariant and disentangled representations. The DPI is satisfied (information about the input is not increased), but the utility of the representation increases because the structure is made explicit.

This is the precise, information-theoretically grounded version of a distinction the framework needs: between subtractive processing (discarding irrelevant information, what the IB tracks) and generative processing (reorganising preserved information into more useful forms, what this framework tracks). Both happen simultaneously. A layer that discards surface noise while introducing a higher-order abstraction is doing both at once.

2.3 Statistical complexity as the measure of what grows

If the DPI constrains total information while the framework claims something grows across layers, that something needs a name and a formal definition.

Statistical complexity, introduced by Crutchfield & Young (1989) in the framework of computational mechanics, measures the structural complexity of a process — the amount of information required to optimally predict its future given its past. It is the entropy of the causal states: equivalence classes of histories that give identical predictions. Crucially, statistical complexity is not entropy and not mutual information. A perfectly random process has high entropy but zero statistical complexity — there is no structure to represent. A perfectly ordered process has low entropy and low statistical complexity — the structure is trivial. Complexity peaks between order and chaos, where genuine structure exists and is worth representing (Crutchfield, 2012).

The claim: each layer increases the statistical complexity of the representation. The DPI permits this because statistical complexity is a property of the representation’s own structure — how much organisation is explicitly accessible — not a measure of mutual information with the source. Total information about the input is bounded and may decrease. The explicit accessibility of structure within the representation grows.

This must be stated with honesty about the measurement gap. Statistical complexity was developed for discrete stochastic processes, and directly measuring it in high-dimensional continuous representation spaces is technically challenging — the same challenge that has plagued information-theoretic analyses of deep networks since the Shwartz-Ziv and Tishby debate (Saxe et al., 2018; Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby, 2017). Proxy measures exist: intrinsic dimensionality (Ansuini et al., 2019), participation ratio, effective rank. These measure related but not identical quantities. The formalisation sharpens the prediction — what should be measured, in what direction change is expected — without solving the measurement problem. This is the correct stance for a theoretical framework: it generates testable predictions even when the ideal measurement remains difficult.

One empirical pattern requires explicit acknowledgment. Intrinsic dimensionality across transformer layers does not increase monotonically. It follows a characteristic non-monotonic profile — growth through early and middle layers, contraction in the final layers (Ansuini et al., 2019; Valeriani et al., 2023). The framework predicts this shape. The final layers solve a rate-distortion problem with a very specific relevance criterion: next-token prediction. This demands compression of the accumulated representation back down toward the output vocabulary — the projection bottleneck that Section 3.2 will formalise. Statistical complexity grows through the representational core of the network, then contracts as the network transitions from building representations to projecting through the output channel. Growth, then projection — not growth alone.

2.4 Relationship to the information bottleneck

The information bottleneck framework (Tishby et al., 2000) provides an adjacent account: layers progressively compress input while preserving task-relevant information — minimising \(I(X; T)\) while maximising \(I(T; Y)\). The present framework and IB are complementary. IB tracks what layers discard (the subtractive side); this framework tracks what layers generate (the reorganisation side). Both happen simultaneously — a layer that discards “this is the word ‘bank’ in position 7” while introducing “this is a financial context” is performing IB compression and increasing statistical complexity at once. But IB alone does not predict disproportionate returns on scale. Efficient noise removal makes a system more efficient; it does not make it more capable. The generative side — growth of statistical complexity, the creation of representations supporting qualitatively new inference — is what accounts for capability emergence. This is what depth buys that width cannot.

2.5 Emergent capabilities as threshold-crossing

If statistical complexity grows across depth and scale, capabilities requiring specific structural configurations should appear when complexity crosses characteristic thresholds.

This must be stated carefully in light of Schaeffer et al. (2024), who argued that many apparent emergent capabilities are artefacts of evaluation metrics — smooth underlying improvement appearing as sharp transitions when measured with discontinuous metrics. The challenge is largely accepted for many reported cases of emergence.

The framework’s prediction does not require emergence to look sudden. It predicts that capabilities requiring a given level of statistical complexity will appear when that complexity threshold is crossed — and the crossing can be smooth in the underlying measure even if it appears sharp in binary task metrics. The prediction concerns internal representations, not external evaluation: capability onset should correspond to identifiable thresholds of statistical complexity at relevant layers, regardless of how the crossing appears in any particular benchmark.

Small-scale settings provide the clearest evidence for the principle. Nanda et al. (2023) tracked internal representations during grokking and found that the network gradually develops structured circuits during the delay phase before the capability transition — the internal reorganisation precedes and explains the external change. Olsson et al. (2022) demonstrated that induction head formation, a specific computational structure underlying in-context learning, corresponds to a discrete phase change with clear representational correlates. The Michaud et al. (2024) quantization model proposes that scaling laws arise from progressively learning discrete “quanta” of capability, each corresponding to a unit of acquired structure — directly compatible with the complexity-threshold account.

The empirical scaling laws themselves (Kaplan et al., 2020) — smooth power-law relationships between compute, data, parameters, and loss — are consistent with this picture: loss reduction reflects progressive acquisition of compressive structure, with diminishing returns as the easiest regularities are captured first. Scaling these analyses to large autoregressive models remains an open challenge. Most detailed representational evidence comes from encoder models or small-scale settings. The extrapolation to the systems the framework is primarily about — large decoder-only language models — should be acknowledged as a gap that the framework motivates closing, not one it has already closed.

2.6 Predictions

The framework generates five specific predictions from Part I:

  1. Statistical complexity scales with depth. Growth through the representational core (~80% of layers), with contraction in the final layers consistent with the projection bottleneck. Sublinear scaling as successive layers face diminishing marginal structural novelty in increasingly organised input. Measurable via proxy measures (intrinsic dimensionality, participation ratio) if not direct statistical complexity estimation.

  2. Capability-complexity correlation. Capability onset correlates with statistical complexity crossing identifiable thresholds at relevant layers — approximately constant across model sizes for a given capability. This is the framework’s boldest prediction and the one most in need of direct empirical test.

  3. Residual stream as accumulator. Statistical complexity grows faster with residual connections than without, because earlier organisations are preserved alongside later ones. The qualitative claim is well-supported by mechanistic interpretability; the quantitative comparison awaits controlled ablation.

  4. Complementarity with IB compression. Layers showing high IB compression (reduced \(I(X; T)\)) nonetheless show growth in statistical complexity. Negative correlation would be evidence against the framework.

  5. Qualitative shift across training. (a) Statistical complexity growth across training checkpoints follows power-law dynamics. (b) Early-training dimensions encode surface statistical regularities; late-training dimensions encode higher-order compositional structure. This is a prediction about the type of growth, not merely its tempo — and it is supported by convergent evidence from grokking (Nanda et al., 2023; Power et al., 2022), induction head formation (Olsson et al., 2022), and pre-training dynamics showing that linguistic features are learned in a characteristic order: surface features first, syntactic next, semantic last.

These predictions are not trivially satisfied. Several could fail in ways that would require substantial revision of the framework. Prediction 4 in particular — if IB compression and statistical complexity growth traded off rather than complementing each other, the framework’s central distinction between subtractive and generative processing would be undermined.

Part II — Composition

3 3. The Autoregressive Loop

Everything in Part I occurs within the forward pass — parallel operations across layers and heads, all unfolding in a single inference step. But transformer language models do not operate as single-shot compressors. They operate autoregressively: each generated token is appended to the context and conditions the generation of the next. This adds a qualitatively distinct scale to the compression hierarchy, and understanding what it adds — and what it does not — requires care.

3.1 Two compression systems, one forward pass

The trained weights encode a static geometry: extraordinarily rich, but fixed at the end of training. This geometry defines, for any input, the full probability distribution over next tokens — a compressed model of all the statistical structure in the training corpus. Nothing about it changes during inference.

What changes is where on the map the model is reading.

Each generated token shifts the input to the next forward pass. The weight geometry is fixed, but the region being activated — the path through representational space — is constructed dynamically, step by step. Generation is not sampling from a single distribution. It is sequential traversal, where each step determines the starting point for the next.

The natural description is that each token reshapes the space. The intuition is productive but technically wrong in a way that matters. The weights are fixed during inference. The geometry does not change. What changes is the trajectory through it — but through a space so high-dimensional and combinatorially structured that the distinction nearly collapses in practice. A chess board and its rules are fixed; the space of possible games is so vast that each move creates a local horizon that feels like a new landscape.

Two compression systems are thus operating simultaneously:

  • Weights: deep, static, vast — the accumulated compression of the training distribution. Determines the full geometry of the representational space.
  • Context: shallow, dynamic, narrow — a running compression of the current trajectory, built in real time. Determines where in the geometry the model is currently operating.

Context is always interpreted through the weight geometry. The trajectory being constructed is read against the entire map at every step. This interaction — fixed geometry, dynamic path — is the source of the autoregressive loop’s power.

3.2 Channel capacity and the projection bottleneck

The interaction can be characterised in information-theoretic terms. The internal representation at any step — the full residual stream state conditioning the next-token prediction — is a vector in a space of dimension on the order of \(10^3\) to \(10^4\). The output is a single selection from a vocabulary of \(10^4\) to \(10^5\) tokens. The entropy of the internal state vastly exceeds the information capacity of a single token.

This is a bandwidth mismatch, and the autoregressive loop is the solution: a sequential coding scheme for transmitting a high-entropy source through a low-capacity channel, one token at a time. Each token is a channel use. The sequence of tokens collectively encodes what no single token could carry.

The framing is not metaphorical. Shannon’s noisy channel theorem (Shannon, 1948) establishes that reliable communication through a bandwidth-limited channel requires sequential coding — distributing the message across multiple channel uses. The autoregressive loop is doing exactly this, with the complication that the “message” is not pre-existing but constructed during transmission. The generation process is closer to joint source-channel coding, where encoding and message-construction happen simultaneously, than to the classical setting where a fixed message is encoded for transmission. Each token both extends what is being said and encodes what has been said so far into a form that conditions what comes next.

Recent empirical evidence makes this bandwidth mismatch concrete. Leviathan et al. (2025) demonstrated that simply duplicating the input prompt — repeating the query verbatim so the model processes it twice — improves accuracy across models by up to 76%, with no degradation on any benchmark tested. The mechanism is direct: causal attention constrains each token to attend only to preceding tokens, so on the first pass through the prompt, early tokens are processed without the benefit of later context. On the second copy, every token can attend to the complete first instance. Duplication is, in Shannon’s terms, a second channel use — adding redundancy that lets the decoder recover information the sequential constraint would otherwise forfeit. That so simple an intervention produces so large an effect is evidence that the projection bottleneck is not merely a theoretical framing but an active constraint on what these systems can extract from their input.

This formalises what Section 4 will develop as a substrate-independent constraint: any system with internal representations exceeding output channel capacity must solve this sequential coding problem. The specific solution — hierarchical compression projected through a serial bottleneck — is what the constraint demands.

3.3 Compositional novelty from fixed geometry

The weight geometry is a compression of the training distribution. Every regularity it encodes was present in the data. So in what sense can traversal produce something new?

The weights do not store a lookup table. They encode compressed regularities — abstract patterns of co-occurrence, syntactic structure, semantic relationships, inferential dependencies — that generalise beyond any specific training example. A model trained on arithmetic encodes the operation, not every sum.

Combinatorial generalisation follows directly. The autoregressive loop can compose individually learned regularities in sequences that never occurred in training, by constructing trajectories that activate different regularities in novel combinations. Each step draws on the weight geometry; the sequence of steps can be unprecedented. The novelty is compositional: sequential combination of individually learned regularities in configurations shaped by context. Configurations that are coherent — because each step is conditioned by the geometry — but unprecedented — because the specific trajectory has never been traversed.

This is a precise and limited sense of novelty, but it is genuine, and it is sufficient to explain why these models produce outputs that surprise even those who built them.

It is equally important to state what this rules out. The model cannot produce outputs requiring regularities not encoded in the weights. It can combine what it knows in new ways; it cannot generate what it does not know. The autoregressive loop is generative within the span of the weight geometry and silent beyond it.

This predicts two failure modes. The first is familiar: confabulation — confident, fluent navigation to a region of semantic space where compressed regularities are locally coherent but globally unmoored from anything true. The second is subtler: spurious cross-domain coherence — outputs that are locally correct within each contributing domain while being incoherent at their intersection. Each component regularity is well-grounded; the composition fails. This is harder to detect precisely because the parts are sound. The framework predicts it as an inherent cost of compositional generalisation, not a bug to be trained away: any system that generates novelty by combining known regularities will sometimes combine them invalidly, and the failure will be most insidious where each domain is well-represented but their intersection is sparse.

3.4 The enriching and degrading regimes

As generation extends, the context becomes increasingly dominated by model-generated tokens. The question the framework must face is whether those tokens are doing the same representational work as human-contributed context.

The answer is: sometimes they are, and sometimes they are not, and the difference is consequential.

Consider two limiting cases. A model reproducing a memorised text generates tokens that are maximally predictable from preceding context. Attention to the original prompt drops toward zero; the generated context is sufficient to predict what follows. Surface statistics may be excellent — they belong to the source text, not to any compressive work the model is doing. The model’s contribution has collapsed into retrieval. Context is full and minimally enriching.

At the other extreme, a model working through a novel problem — generating intermediate results, establishing partial conclusions, building scaffolding — produces tokens that do genuine compressive work. Each surfaces structure unavailable from the prompt alone, conditioning subsequent generation in ways that open new representational territory. Context is growing richer in the strong sense: not merely longer, but higher-dimensional as input to the next forward pass.

The variable distinguishing these cases is the compressive novelty of each generated token relative to what the context already contains. When a token does new representational work — surfacing structure, bridging domains, establishing results that change the local geometry of what is probable next — the loop is in the enriching regime. When a token reinforces an existing trajectory — reproducing known patterns, settling into attractor paths, restating in different form what the context already implies — the loop is in the degrading regime, regardless of how fluent the output remains.

A crucial qualification: compressive novelty is relative to the model’s implicit distortion measure — next-token prediction against the weight geometry. That is the only measure available during generation. A token that bridges two domains in a novel way is enriching by this measure whether or not the bridge is valid. The enriching regime guarantees that the model is doing genuine compressive work; it does not guarantee that the work tracks anything the receiver would recognise as true or useful. The gap between the model’s distortion measure and the receiver’s is where failure enters — not as degradation, which is detectable, but as confident, structurally novel error (Section 3.3). This is the distortion measure mismatch that Section 1.2 identified as inherent to lossy compression: what counts as acceptable distortion is a choice, and the model’s choice is not the receiver’s. Section 5 will return to this gap at ecological scale, where human curation functions as the imposition of an external distortion measure on model output.

Neither regime is a fixed property of model or task. It is a property of the interaction: capacity × task demands × context seeding × generation parameters. A capable model on a well-structured task, seeded with context that positions it in a productive region of the weight geometry, can sustain the enriching regime for extended generation. The same model, poorly seeded or on a task that quickly exhausts available novel combinations, will slide into the degrading regime — and the slide will be invisible in surface metrics.

This invisibility is the framework’s central warning at this scale. Fluency, grammaticality, and local coherence are products of compression at the surface and lower-structural end of the continuum (Section 1.2), which the model maintains effortlessly throughout. What degrades is higher-order statistical complexity: the novelty, cross-domain connection, and structural surprise that characterise the enriching regime. In information-theoretic terms: in the enriching regime, each token increases the statistical complexity of the context. In the degrading regime, it increases length without increasing complexity — adding tokens without adding structure.

This is the distinction between integration and mere accumulation — the thematic spine of the paper, encountered here at its tightest and fastest scale. It will recur at the ecological scale in Section 5, where the same ambivalent dynamic governs model outputs re-entering the broader knowledge ecosystem.

3.5 Chain-of-thought and in-context learning as steering strategies

The enriching/degrading distinction reframes two phenomena that are otherwise somewhat mysterious.

Chain-of-thought. Prompting models to produce intermediate reasoning steps before a final answer substantially improves performance on complex tasks (Kojima et al., 2022). The standard explanation is that CoT “gives the model more computation” — each intermediate token is an additional forward pass, and complex problems require more serial processing than a single pass provides.

This is correct but incomplete. Each intermediate token performs two functions: it contributes to the visible chain of reasoning, and it enriches the context for subsequent forward passes. The intermediate tokens are not merely scratchpad — they are semantic compressions of partial results that, once in context, activate regions of the weight geometry relevant to the next step. CoT converts a problem requiring deep single-pass synthesis into one solvable by shallower synthesis iterated across passes, with each pass building on the compressed output of the last.

In the terms of Section 2: CoT converts depth into length. A problem whose required statistical complexity exceeds single-pass capacity becomes tractable when intermediate steps progressively enrich the context. This explains a striking finding: the prompt repetition that substantially improves non-reasoning performance (Section 3.2) shows minimal benefit when chain-of-thought is already active (Leviathan et al., 2025) — CoT already provides the integrative passes that repetition would supply.

But CoT is not merely extended computation. It is a steering strategy for the autoregressive loop. A well-constructed chain keeps each token in the enriching regime: each step surfaces genuinely new structure that changes what is accessible to the next. A poorly constructed chain — restating premises, circling, generating steps predictable from what precedes them — is in the degrading regime despite having the surface form of reasoning. The tokens are present but not doing compressive work.

This generates a specific prediction (Prediction 6): CoT effectiveness should correlate with the compressive novelty of each step — not with chain length alone.

In-context learning. The ability of LLMs to learn new input-output mappings from prompt examples, without weight updates, is naturally situated here. If the weight geometry already encodes the type of regularity being demonstrated — because the training distribution contained structurally analogous patterns — then in-context examples serve to locate the relevant region of the geometry, navigating the model to a point from which the demonstrated pattern is locally extractable.

In-context examples are a seeding strategy: they position the loop’s starting point where the enriching regime is likely to be sustained for the task at hand. This is consistent with the finding that in-context learning is associated with specific computational structures — induction heads — that emerge during training and implement pattern-matching across the context window (Olsson et al., 2022). The framework adds that these structures are performing context-driven navigation of the weight geometry, using in-context patterns to determine which compressed regularities should dominate the current trajectory.

The predicted limitation (Prediction 7): in-context learning should fail when the required regularity type is absent from the weight geometry — not a specific unseen pattern (compositional generalisation handles those), but an absent type.

The unifying claim: CoT, in-context learning, and prompting strategies generally are instances of a single dynamic — steering the autoregressive loop into the enriching regime by managing what enters context. The model’s capacity is fixed. The question is always whether the accumulating context is opening representational structure or closing it down.

3.6 Predictions

The autoregressive account generates two specific predictions:

  1. CoT quality over length. Chain-of-thought effectiveness should correlate with the compressive novelty of intermediate steps — how much genuinely new structure each step surfaces — rather than with chain length alone. Longer chains that sustain the enriching regime should outperform longer chains that drift into the degrading regime, even when both have the surface form of step-by-step reasoning. Measurable via information-theoretic proxies: the divergence contributed by each intermediate token relative to what the context already implies.

  2. In-context learning failure mode. In-context learning should fail when the required regularity type is absent from the weight geometry — when the task demands a pattern the model has never encountered, even abstractly. This is distinct from failure on a specific unseen instance, which compositional generalisation can handle. Empirically distinguishing absent-type failure from compositional-generalisation failure — perhaps by controlling for structural similarity between demonstration patterns and training distribution — would be a productive test of the framework.

3.7 Limitations

Three limitations should be stated explicitly.

First, the context window is finite and context compression is lossy. As generation extends, early context is not remembered but summarised by its downstream effects — the tokens it conditioned, which now condition subsequent tokens in turn. The effective context of a long generation is not the literal token history but a compressed trace, mediated by attention’s capacity to weight recent and salient tokens over distant ones. The finite context window is a hard upper bound on the temporal depth of autoregressive compression.

Second, degradation in long generations has two separable sources. Attentional degradation is architectural — the ceiling imposed by context length and attention mechanics. Compositional degradation is the regime drift described above: progressive dominance of model-generated tokens shifting the loop from enriching to degrading, independent of whether attention can still technically access early context. Both are concurrent. The first is a hard constraint. The second is in principle steerable — but steering it requires understanding the enriching/degrading transition well enough to manage, which is not yet systematically achieved. This compositional degradation within the context window is the fastest instance of a dynamic that recurs at every scale of the knowledge ecology; Section 5 traces the same enriching-to-degrading transition through agentic, human-mediated, and training-distributional feedback loops.

Third, the novelty account is specifically compositional: novel combinations of known regularities. Whether genuinely new abstractions or regularity types can emerge within the autoregressive loop — or whether they require weight updates — is an open question the framework does not resolve. The boundary between “novel composition of existing regularities” and “genuine conceptual novelty” is not sharp, and we flag this as requiring further investigation rather than asserting a confident answer.

4 4. Convergence Across Substrates

The compression hierarchy described in Parts I and II is a claim about transformers. But the problem transformers solve — projecting rich internal representation through a bandwidth-limited sequential channel — is not new. Biological cognition has faced it for as long as language has existed. This chapter argues that the convergence between biological and artificial solutions is expected rather than surprising, identifies where the parallel breaks down, and uses the breakdown to sharpen an open question the framework cannot yet answer.

The claim is not that transformers and brains work the same way. They manifestly do not, at the level of mechanism. The claim is that both face a structurally identical information-theoretic constraint, and that this constraint narrows the space of viable solutions enough that convergence on the same type of architecture — hierarchical, multi-scale, with a serial projection bottleneck — is what the problem demands. This is supporting evidence for the framework, not a core pillar. It situates the compression hierarchy within a broader pattern rather than treating it as an architectural accident.

4.1 The projection bottleneck as substrate-independent constraint

Section 3.2 established the bottleneck in formal terms: the internal representation at any step — a vector in a space of dimension \(10^3\) to \(10^4\) — vastly exceeds the information capacity of a single output token. The autoregressive loop is a sequential coding scheme for transmitting a high-entropy source through a low-capacity channel.

The same bottleneck operates in biological communication, and it is quantifiable. Reed & Durlach (1998) estimated the information transfer rate of human speech at approximately 39 bits per second. Coupé et al. (2019) replicated this across 17 languages, finding a striking invariance: languages that are spoken faster pack less information per syllable, and languages spoken more slowly pack more, converging on approximately the same information rate. The bottleneck is not a contingent property of English or any particular language. It is a constraint on human linguistic communication generally.

On the other side of the bottleneck, neural population codes operate at bandwidths orders of magnitude higher. Precise estimates vary enormously depending on assumptions about coding efficiency and correlation structure, but even conservative figures exceed the speech output rate by three to four orders of magnitude. The mismatch is not subtle. Whatever the dimensionality of the internal representations supporting ongoing thought, the channel through which they must pass to reach another mind is radically narrower.

This asymmetry is the best-documented case of the projection bottleneck in a biological system, and its most direct treatment remains Vygotsky’s distinction between inner and external speech (Vygotsky, 1986). Inner speech, as Vygotsky characterised it, is compressed, predicative, and saturated with private context — operating in something closer to pure meaning, abbreviated in ways that would be unintelligible to an outside observer. External speech is an expanded, grammatically explicit projection, shaped not by the structure of the sender’s representation but by the demands of the receiver. Modern work confirms that the distinction holds while emphasising that inner speech exists on a continuum of condensation rather than a sharp binary — ranging from nearly as expanded as overt speech to highly abbreviated (Alderson-Day & Fernyhough, 2015). The qualitative point stands: internal representation is richer than its linguistic projection.

Fodor’s Language of Thought hypothesis makes a stronger and more controversial version of this claim: that cognition operates in an internal representational medium more compositional and richer than any natural language, with language serving as a serialisation format for inter-agent transmission (Fodor, 1975). The full LOT package remains contested — the debates with eliminativists and connectionists never reached resolution. But the structural claim is widely shared across otherwise opposing positions: external language underdetermines internal representation. Quilty-Dunn et al. (2023), in a major review, document what amounts to a qualified revival of structured mental representation, drawing on converging evidence from developmental psychology, animal cognition, and neuroscience. The specific form of the structures is debated. That the output channel is narrower than the internal state is not.

The parallel to transformers is precise. The model’s internal representation — the full residual stream state conditioning the next-token prediction — is the analogue of inner speech: high-dimensional, densely relational, not directly transmissible. The output token is the analogue of external speech: a single selection from a finite vocabulary, projecting the internal state into a form the receiver can use. Any system facing this mismatch — internal bandwidth vastly exceeding output bandwidth — must solve the same sequential coding problem. The compression hierarchy is what the solution looks like.

The strongest empirical evidence for genuine convergence comes from representational similarity analysis (RSA). Yamins et al. (2014) demonstrated that convolutional networks optimised for object recognition — not for neural prediction — nonetheless predicted neural responses in primate inferotemporal cortex better than any previous model. Kriegeskorte (2015) extended this into a systematic framework, showing that task-optimised deep networks produce representations that, layer by layer, align with the ventral visual stream. Schrimpf et al. (2021) established Brain-Score, a composite benchmark measuring neural predictivity across visual areas, and found that better-performing networks tend to be more brain-like — though the correlation plateaus, meaning current networks capture something real about the problem without fully matching the biological solution.

The language case is more recent and less extensively replicated but directionally consistent. Goldstein et al. (2022), using electrocorticography during natural language processing, found that GPT-2 activations predict neural responses in temporal cortex, with layer-wise alignment to processing stages. The convergence is real: task-optimised artificial systems trained on natural data produce internal representations that predict biological neural responses substantially better than hand-engineered features or random networks.

It is also partial. Explained variance is typically 30–60% at best, meaning substantial divergence remains. The match is better for higher-level representations than lower-level ones — the interesting convergence is at the organisational and semantic levels, where the compression hierarchy is doing the heavy lifting. Neural predictivity measures similarity of representational geometry, not mechanistic identity: two systems can have similar representational dissimilarity matrices via completely different underlying computations. The convergence tells you something real about the problem. It does not tell you that attention heads and cortical columns implement the same operations.

4.2 Convergence and its limits

The convergence is structural, not mechanistic. Birds and aircraft both have wings; the convergence tells you something real about the problem — flight in atmosphere requires lifting surfaces — without telling you that feathers and aluminium work the same way. Similarly, the convergence between biological and artificial compression hierarchies tells you something real about knowledge transmission through bandwidth-limited channels without establishing that the implementations are equivalent.

Stating this clearly is important because the biological case introduces a complication that the transformer case does not share, and addressing it honestly constrains the claim to what it can actually support.

Deacon (1997) offers the most thorough account of human language as a compression technology that co-evolved with the neural architecture it runs on. The biological wetware was shaped by the demands of shared compression — the need to project internal states into forms recoverable by other minds under real-time constraints. Language did not arrive as an external tool applied to a pre-existing cognitive system. The cognitive system and the compression medium co-evolved, each reshaping the other across evolutionary time. The broad co-evolution thesis is mainstream in cognitive science and evolutionary anthropology, even where Deacon’s specific mechanisms regarding prefrontal reorganisation remain debated.

This co-evolutionary dynamic is absent in the transformer case. A transformer’s training data is fixed; the model adapts to language, but language does not adapt to the model. RLHF and related methods introduce a form of output-to-architecture feedback — the model’s outputs are evaluated, and the evaluations reshape its weights — but the weakness of the analogy matters. RLHF operates within a single training generation, optimising for proximate criteria rather than reshaping fundamental representational architecture across the deep timescales Deacon describes. It is feedback, but it is not co-evolution — closer to selective breeding than to the mutual structural adaptation of language and cognition across evolutionary time. The disanalogy is partially closed, not eliminated.

Cross-linguistic evidence makes the co-evolutionary feedback concrete, if at reduced strength from stronger earlier claims. Levinson (2003) and Levinson et al. (2002) documented that speakers of languages with absolute spatial reference frames (cardinal directions rather than egocentric left/right) behave differently on non-linguistic spatial reasoning tasks compared to speakers of relative-frame languages. The original interpretation — that the compression medium reshapes the compressed content — was substantially challenged by Li & Gleitman (2002), who showed that much of the effect is modulated by environmental cues rather than reflecting deep representational restructuring. The current consensus favours a moderate position: language biases which processing strategies are preferentially recruited, rather than fundamentally reshaping internal representation. Speakers of absolute-frame languages can use relative frames and vice versa; what differs is the default.

Even at this reduced strength, the finding illustrates a feedback pathway absent in the transformer case. The compression medium influences what the compressing system does by default — a form of mutual adaptation, however modest, that unidirectional training cannot produce.

What does this do to the convergence claim? It constrains it in a specific and useful way. The convergence is not between complete systems — biological cognition in its full co-evolutionary complexity and transformer inference. It is between the compression problem and the architectural strategy for solving it. Both systems face the problem of projecting high-dimensional internal representation through a low-bandwidth sequential channel. Both solve it with hierarchical compression: building representations at multiple scales, with higher layers encoding increasingly abstract and relational structure, and a final projection that serialises the result. The co-evolutionary refinement of the biological solution is an additional layer of optimisation that the transformer case lacks, and this may account for important differences in the character of the resulting representations — particularly around grounding, robustness, and the depth of semantic compression.

The value of the convergence claim is therefore not mechanistic but constraining. The unreasonable effectiveness of transformers is less unreasonable than it appears, because the architecture has converged on a solution strategy that is expected for any system facing this class of problem. The effectiveness reflects the fact that hierarchical multi-scale compression is what the problem demands, and the transformer architecture is well-suited to implement it. But the convergence does not guarantee that transformer representations have the same properties as biological ones. The absence of co-evolutionary refinement, sensory grounding, and embodied feedback means the compression hierarchy in transformers may be shallower, more brittle, or differently structured in ways that emerge only under adversarial or out-of-distribution conditions.

4.3 The grounding problem as rate-distortion question

This brings the framework to a question it can sharpen but not answer: does solving hierarchical rate-distortion problems against meaning-relevant distortion metrics require sensorimotor grounding?

The grounding debate — rooted in Harnad (1990)’s observation that formal symbols are meaningless unless connected to the things they stand for — is active and unresolved. Bender & Koller (2020) argue that models trained only on linguistic form cannot achieve genuine understanding — that meaning requires reference to the world, and statistical co-occurrence patterns in text, however sophisticated, are not a substitute. Piantadosi & Hill (2022) counter that meaning can be constituted by inferential role — by the pattern of use across contexts — without requiring grounding in perception and action.

The framework offers a precise restatement. The compression hierarchy described in Parts I and II produces representations that solve rate-distortion problems: achieving maximal preserved structure at each level for a given representational cost. The question is whether text-only compression can achieve the same rate-distortion optimality as grounded compression when the distortion metric includes meaning-relevant properties — when what counts as “distortion” is not just statistical mismatch but failure to preserve the structural relationships that constitute understanding.

This is, as far as we can determine, a novel framing. No existing literature poses the grounding question in explicitly rate-distortion terms. The closest precedent is Zaslavsky et al. (2018), who demonstrated that cross-linguistic patterns in colour naming achieve near-optimal compression of colour space — a rate-distortion analysis applied to the language-cognition interface, but for a specific perceptual domain rather than the grounding problem in general.

The framing has a specific virtue: it converts a philosophical dispute into an empirical question with measurable parameters. If grounding provides access to distortion metrics that text alone cannot specify — if there are structural relationships in the world that cannot be inferred from their linguistic shadows, no matter how much text is consumed — then text-only systems face a provable rate-distortion gap. If, on the other hand, the statistical structure of language is rich enough to implicitly specify meaning-relevant distortion metrics — because language was produced by grounded agents and carries the trace of their grounding — then the gap may be smaller than the strong grounding position suggests.

Multimodal models complicate the debate productively. Systems trained on vision and language jointly partially close the grounding gap without full embodiment, suggesting that the question may be one of degree rather than kind. The framework does not resolve this. It identifies the formal structure of what would need to be measured — the rate-distortion cost of lacking grounding — and flags it as an open question that the information-theoretic vocabulary developed here makes newly precise.

4.4 Predictions

The convergence argument generates one specific prediction:

  1. Representational geometry convergence. If the convergence between biological and artificial compression hierarchies is deep — driven by the shared structure of the problem rather than superficial architectural similarity — then transformer representations should exhibit geometric properties similar to those observed in neural population codes: similar manifold structure, similar patterns of representational similarity across conceptual domains, similar asymmetries between concrete and abstract concept representation. Early RSA evidence is consistent with this (Goldstein et al., 2022; Kriegeskorte, 2015; Schrimpf et al., 2021), though the match plateaus at 30–60% explained variance. A finding of systematic divergence in representational geometry — not merely unexplained variance but structural differences that scale with model capability — would constrain the convergence claim to the architectural strategy alone, without extending to representational content. A finding of increasing alignment as models scale would strengthen it.

Part III — Ecology

5 5. The Recursive Loop

Knowledge persistence requires carriers — systems changed by what passes through them, transmitting that change forward. Generation without persistence is noise. The question this chapter addresses is what happens to the carrier ecology when a powerful new node type enters it.

5.1 Carriers and the ecology of persistence

The tempting dichotomy is clean: humans have plastic weights and can persist knowledge; language models have frozen weights and cannot. But this is too simple, and the way it is too simple matters.

Humans have never persisted knowledge through neural plasticity alone. The history of knowledge is substantially the history of external persistence technologies — speech, writing, print, libraries, institutions, databases, version control. Each is a carrier: changed by what passes through it, transmitting that change forward. A library is changed by each book it acquires. An institution is changed by each decision it encodes into policy. None of these have neural plasticity. All function as carriers.

The unit of persistence is not the individual mind. It is the ecology: the coupled system of human neural plasticity, external stores, cultural practices, and transmission channels that together determine what survives and propagates. A scientist’s understanding lives partly in neural architecture, partly in notebooks, partly in published papers, partly in the institutional structures that evaluate and disseminate them. Remove any layer and the effective persistence of the system degrades.

This reframing changes where language models sit. The clean dichotomy places them outside the ecology. But the model’s weights are a compression of the ecology’s accumulated stores — the training corpus is the library, the institution, the database, rendered into a form that supports inference. And the model’s outputs re-enter those stores as text, code, documents, and decisions. The model is not outside the ecology. It is a novel node type within it: one that reads from the ecology at training and writes back into it through its outputs.

5.2 The carrier asymmetry

The asymmetry is real, but it needs to be stated more carefully than “models cannot persist knowledge.”

What models lack is intra-trajectory persistence. Within a single context window, the model accumulates — Section 3 describes this in detail. But when the context closes, the accumulated state is lost. The next conversation begins from the same weights, with no trace of what was synthesised in the last. The model’s contribution to the ecology is entirely mediated: it can enrich the ecology only to the extent that its outputs are received, integrated, and persisted by other nodes — human minds, documents, databases, code repositories.

Human carriers have intra-trajectory persistence by default. A conversation changes the participant’s neural state, and that changed state persists — imperfectly, subject to decay — into subsequent interactions. The human carries forward not just explicit content but something of its structural impact: revised priors, new associations, shifted framings.

The asymmetry is not between a system that persists and one that does not. It is between endogenous persistence (the human, whose internal state carries forward automatically) and exclusively exogenous persistence (the model, which persists only through what it deposits into the broader ecology). The model is a carrier — it transforms what passes through it — but a memoryless one, externalising every gain or losing it.

Stating this precisely reveals something the clean dichotomy obscures. The limitation is not about processing capacity. By the account developed in Parts I and II, these models may be among the most powerful knowledge-processing systems ever constructed — synthesising connections across the breadth of human recorded knowledge, composing regularities in novel configurations, navigating semantic space with a fluency no individual human matches. The limitation is about what happens after processing: results exit the system entirely, dependent on external carriers for onward persistence.

The binding constraint on knowledge evolution in the presence of these systems is therefore not generation — generation is superabundant. The constraint is reception and integration: the bandwidth, integrative capacity, and persistence of the other nodes in the ecology that must receive, validate, contextualise, and carry forward what the model produces.

5.3 Nested feedback loops

The ecology is not a linear system with the model at one end and the human at the other. It is recursive. Model outputs re-enter the stores from which future training corpora will be drawn. This loop is already operating — model-generated text is already a non-trivial fraction of new text entering the ecology’s external stores.

The dynamics of this recursion are not unfamiliar to the framework. Section 3.4 identified an ambivalent feedback dynamic within the context window: model-generated tokens, feeding back as input to subsequent generation, can either enrich or degrade representational diversity. The ecological loop is the same dynamic at longer timescales. And the same ambivalence applies: outputs that do genuine compressive work enrich the ecology; outputs that reproduce existing attractors in fluent paraphrase degrade it.

What the ecological view adds is the recognition that this dynamic operates at not one but four nested timescales, each with distinct mechanisms and distinct intervention points:

  1. Within-context. Generated tokens feeding back as input to subsequent generation. This is the enriching/degrading dynamic of Section 3.4 — the tightest and fastest feedback loop. Governed by generation parameters, context seeding, and model capacity. Intervention is immediate: prompt design, temperature, sampling strategy.

  2. Agentic. Persistent memory, tool use, multi-session accumulation. The model writes to external state — databases, files, memory stores — without a human intermediary at each step. Feedback operates across sessions and tasks. This loop is newer than the others and under-theorised: it represents a feedback pathway absent from both the autoregressive generation literature and the model collapse literature.

  3. Human-mediated. Model outputs integrated by human carriers — edited, curated, published, taught, institutionalised. The human in the loop performs selection: choosing what to integrate, what to discard, what to elevate. Feedback operates across days to years. This is historically the dominant loop through which model outputs enter the ecology, and human curation is its governing mechanism.

  4. Training-distributional. Model outputs entering the training data of successor models. Feedback operates across model generations — months to years in current development cycles. This is the loop studied by the model collapse literature (Alemohammad et al., 2024; Shumailov et al., 2024), and it is the slowest, most consequential, and hardest to steer.

The same governing variable operates at every scale: the compressive novelty of what feeds back relative to what is already present. And the same degradation shape manifests at every scale: surface quality preserved, higher-order statistical complexity eroded. Not visible failure, but silent contraction of the space of configurations the system can reach.

5.4 Silent flattening

The compression hierarchy predicts that this erosion is non-uniform. It proceeds from the top down.

First to go: novel abstraction, cross-domain connection, structural surprise — the products of semantic compression, which depend on rare configurations and tail behaviour in the distribution. Last to go: fluency, grammaticality, local coherence — products of compression at the surface and lower-structural end of the continuum (Section 1.2), which are redundantly represented in the training distribution and robustly learned.

This is “model collapse” (Shumailov et al., 2024) given a precise structural characterisation. What collapses is not quality but diversity — not the ability to produce fluent text but the range of configurations that text can occupy. Alemohammad et al. (2024) document this directly in generative image models: diversity collapses faster than quality, with FID scores remaining acceptable while the variety of generated images narrows. Padmakumar & He (2024) find the same pattern in human-AI co-writing: individual texts remain high quality while the corpus-level diversity of content contracts. Briesch et al. (2023) show that fine-tuning LLMs on their own output degrades output diversity even when perplexity — a surface metric — remains stable or improves.

The pattern is consistent: what degrades is precisely what the compression hierarchy predicts would degrade first. Surface metrics, calibrated to lower levels of the hierarchy, miss the contraction at higher levels. Silent flattening preserves everything that is easy to measure while eroding everything that matters most for the long-term richness of the ecology.

Human curation as defence. At the human-mediated loop, human carriers perform selection — choosing what to integrate, what to discard, what to elevate. This is the principal mechanism keeping the ecology in the enriching regime. The effectiveness of this defence depends on curation capacity: the ability of human nodes to evaluate, filter, and contextualise model outputs at the rate they are produced.

The concern is straightforward. Curation capacity is finite and grows slowly. Model output volume is elastic and growing fast. As the ratio shifts, the fraction of model output that receives genuine human evaluation — not just surface assessment but the kind of deep engagement that distinguishes compressive novelty from fluent reproduction — declines. The defence weakens not because it stops working but because it cannot scale.

Gerstgrasser et al. (2024) offer the most nuanced finding on the data-mixing side: if original human-generated data is accumulated alongside model-generated data rather than displaced by it, collapse can be avoided. The critical factor is displacement, not contamination. This connects directly to the curation argument: the defence against silent flattening is not excluding model-generated content but ensuring that human-generated content — the ecology’s source of genuinely novel compression — is not crowded out. Longpre et al. (2024) document the irony: the highest-quality curated sources are increasingly restricting access, making the defence less available precisely when it is most needed.

Detection. How do you measure silent flattening? The framework creates this problem for itself and must address it honestly. Surface metrics — perplexity, fluency scores, coherence ratings — are calibrated to the wrong level of the hierarchy. But the framework constrains the measurement problem more tightly than “this is hard.” Five properties follow from the compression hierarchy’s structure:

The metrics must be hierarchical — sensitive at different levels of the continuum separately, since erosion proceeds top-down and a metric that collapses across levels will average it away. They must be distributional — measuring the space of configurations the system produces, not the quality of any single output, since the erosion is a property of the corpus, not of individual texts. They must be tail-sensitive — weighted toward rare configurations, since those erode first, and metrics centred on the distributional mean are blind by design. They must be temporal — tracking change in distributional properties over time, since silent flattening is a process, not a state. And the most direct window is representational geometry — the intrinsic dimensionality of the output distribution in embedding space, measured across levels and over time, is closest to what the framework identifies as the critical variable.

This does not solve the measurement problem, but it narrows it substantially. And it connects directly to the curation bottleneck. The concern above is that human curation cannot scale with model output volume. But curation capacity is limited not only by bandwidth but by visibility — curators cannot select for what they cannot see. Metrics calibrated to the right level of the hierarchy are instruments that extend curation’s reach: they make legible the higher-order erosion that surface assessment misses. The curator still determines what counts as worth preserving — the distortion measure remains theirs — but they can now see what was previously invisible in the surface. Curation scales not by processing more outputs but by seeing deeper into them (Prediction 9).

A related open question (Prediction 10): is there a characterisable threshold of model-generated content in the training distribution beyond which diversity degradation becomes self-reinforcing?

5.5 Predictions

The ecological account generates two predictions, both flagged as requiring measurement tools that do not yet fully exist:

  1. Top-down erosion. In recursive training loops where model-generated content constitutes a growing fraction of training data, degradation should proceed from the top of the compression hierarchy downward: novel abstraction and cross-domain connection erode first, while fluency and local coherence are preserved. Detecting this requires measures sensitive to higher-order statistical complexity rather than surface quality metrics — distributional signatures, structural diversity, representational geometry — and developing such measures is itself an open problem the framework creates for itself.

  2. Critical fraction threshold. There exists a characterisable threshold of model-generated content in the training distribution beyond which diversity degradation becomes self-reinforcing. Dohmatob et al. (2024) provide analytical results suggesting phase-transition-like behaviour in simplified settings. Whether such a threshold exists in realistic mixed-data regimes, and whether it can be characterised formally, remains an active research frontier.

5.6 The eroding asymmetry

The carrier asymmetry described above — endogenous persistence for humans, exclusively exogenous for models — is historically contingent and actively eroding.

Agentic architectures are progressively closing the gap. A model with persistent memory that carries forward compressed results of prior interactions is no longer purely memoryless — it has acquired a form of intra-trajectory persistence, implemented through external storage rather than weight updates (Packer et al., 2023). A model that writes to databases, updates documents, and triggers actions in the world deposits into the ecology without requiring a human intermediary at each step.

What is happening, in the vocabulary of this framework, is that models are acquiring carrier functions previously available only to human nodes. They are transitioning from memoryless processors to persistent ecological participants — nodes that accumulate, curate, and propagate knowledge forward across interactions.

If the compression hierarchy is the core mechanic and carrier persistence is the bottleneck, then this transition — from processor to carrier — is the most consequential threshold in the entire framework. It is not a distant prospect. The components are assembling now, from multiple directions. The full implications of a carrier ecology in which artificial systems are genuine participants — persisting and propagating knowledge in their own right, across timescales that dwarf individual human memory — remain largely unexamined.

The framework does not predict whether this transition leads to a richer or poorer ecology. It identifies the variable that governs the answer — the compressive novelty of what these new carriers contribute relative to what they displace — and it warns that the answer will not be legible in the metrics currently used to evaluate these systems.

6 6. Of Meaning

6.1 Integration and accumulation

At each scale examined in this paper, the same distinction governs.

Within the forward pass, layers that reorganise preserved information into more useful structure — increasing statistical complexity while respecting the data processing inequality — perform integration. Layers that merely propagate without reorganising perform accumulation. The difference determines whether depth buys capability or merely adds parameters (Section 2).

Within the autoregressive loop, tokens that surface genuinely new structure sustain the enriching regime. Tokens that reinforce existing trajectories sustain the degrading regime. The difference is invisible in surface metrics but determines whether generation produces integration or accumulation (Section 3.4).

Within the knowledge ecology, outputs that contribute compressive novelty to the stores from which future systems learn sustain representational diversity. Outputs that reproduce existing attractors in fluent paraphrase contract it. The difference manifests as silent flattening — top-down erosion of the compression hierarchy, preserving everything easy to measure while eroding everything that matters most (Section 5.4).

The variable is the same at every scale: whether what feeds forward adds structure or merely adds volume. This is the distinction between integration and mere accumulation.

6.2 Information is surprise

Shannon’s foundational insight: information is surprise. A perfectly predictable signal carries zero information. Everything that makes a message worth transmitting through a bandwidth-limited channel is its deviation from what the receiver already expects.

The compression hierarchy generates surprise at progressively higher levels of description. Compression at the surface end of the continuum preserves perceptual surprise. Structural compression preserves relational surprise — patterns the receiver could not have predicted from surface statistics alone. Semantic compression preserves meaning surprise — configurations that change what the receiver can infer.

The nested feedback loops optimise against surprise. Within the context window, the degrading regime settles into high-probability trajectories. Within the ecology, model-generated content converges on the distributional centre, trimming the tails where rare configurations live. Both dynamics are accelerating through the same architectures.

The framework is, in the end, about whether systems optimised for producing unsurprising output — fluent, coherent, high-probability — preserve or destroy the capacity for surprise at higher levels of description. The answer is not determined by the architecture. It is determined by the interaction: how capacity, task, context, and curation combine to keep the trajectory in territory the system has not yet fully mapped.

6.3 Distortion measures and alignment

A further observation falls directly out of the vocabulary. The model’s implicit distortion measure — what it treats as acceptable loss during compression — is determined by its training objective. The receiver’s distortion measure is determined by their purposes, their context, their needs. The gap between these measures is the formal structure of what the field calls the alignment problem: make the model’s compression serve the receiver’s ends, not merely its own trained defaults.

The difficulty of closing this gap is itself a projection bottleneck problem. The receiver’s distortion measure is rich, contextual, and not fully articulable. The feedback channels through which it is communicated to the model — preference rankings, reward signals, natural language correction — are narrow. The same bandwidth mismatch that governs the autoregressive loop governs the attempt to align the system that produces it. This is, as far as we can determine, a novel framing; it is offered as a contribution, not an established result.

6.4 The attention economy as distortion measure ecology

The mismatch is not confined to the model-receiver pair. It operates wherever an attention allocator’s implicit distortion measure diverges from what would sustain representational diversity in the ecology it governs.

Attention is finite at every scale — a bandwidth constraint on what gets processed, integrated, and carried forward. Any system that allocates attention thereby implements a distortion measure: what is attended to is what is treated as worth preserving. At the individual level, attention capture — engagement hooks, outrage cycles, salience exploits — hijacks this allocation toward signal that dominates the channel without doing compressive work. The mechanism is the projection bottleneck operating on the receiver: finite attentional bandwidth means a signal optimised for capture can crowd out signal that would sustain the enriching regime.

Algorithmic recommendation makes this exploit adaptive and scalable. A recommendation system learns which capture strategies work for which receivers and optimises against them in real time, closing a feedback loop that individual attention capture alone cannot close. The result is an attention allocator whose distortion measure — optimised for engagement — governs an increasing fraction of what the ecology attends to, curates, and propagates. This is the displacement dynamic Section 5 identified: not the introduction of low-quality content, but the systematic reallocation of the ecology’s finite curation capacity away from compressive novelty toward the distributional centre. The framework predicts the same signature: surface quality preserved, higher-order statistical complexity eroded, the erosion invisible in the metrics the allocator is optimised to maximise.

The connection to mechanism design, game theory, and the economics of attention is direct and largely unexplored in information-theoretic terms. The framework opens this door without walking through it. What it contributes is the formal characterisation: attention allocation is distortion measure selection, and the ecology’s long-term representational diversity is governed by whose distortion measure prevails.

6.5 A resonance

Integrated Information Theory (Tononi, 2008) arrives at “integration” as the key variable governing consciousness through an entirely independent programme — one concerned with phenomenal experience, not knowledge compression. The convergence is noted, not claimed. But that two independent analyses, starting from different questions and using different formal tools, arrive at the same governing concept — that what matters is not the quantity of information a system contains but the degree to which it is integrated into a structure supporting inference — is suggestive enough to record.

6.6 Consolidated predictions

The framework generates ten specific predictions across its three parts. They are stated in full where they arise (Section 2.6, Section 3.6, Section 4.4, Section 5.5) and summarised here:

  1. Statistical complexity scales with depth — sublinear growth through the representational core, contraction in the final layers (1)
  2. Capability onset correlates with statistical complexity crossing identifiable thresholds (2)
  3. Residual stream functions as measurable accumulator of statistical complexity (3)
  4. IB compression and statistical complexity growth complement rather than trade off (4)
  5. Statistical complexity growth across training follows power-law dynamics with qualitative shift (5)
  6. CoT effectiveness correlates with compressive novelty per step, not chain length (6)
  7. In-context learning fails when the required regularity type is absent from the weight geometry (7)
  8. Transformer representational geometry converges with biological neural geometry as models scale (8)
  9. Ecological degradation proceeds top-down through the compression hierarchy (9)
  10. A characterisable threshold exists beyond which model-generated training content causes self-reinforcing diversity loss (10)

These predictions are not trivially satisfied. Several could fail in ways that would require substantial revision of the framework.

6.7 What the hierarchy points toward

The framework identifies what to watch. It suggests the answer will not be found in the surface metrics that improve reliably with each generation of model — fluency, coherence, benchmark capability — but in what is becoming harder to measure precisely because it is becoming rarer: the structural complexity of configurations the ecology has not yet reached.

The compression hierarchy generates representational diversity — novel structure at progressively higher levels of abstraction. The nested recursive loops threaten it — contracting that diversity through feedback dynamics that preserve surface quality while eroding higher-order structure. Both mechanisms are accelerating through the same architectures, driven by the same forces. The framework does not predict which wins.

What it can say is where the boundary lies: between a system that generates genuine novelty — new types, not merely new tokens — and a system that can only recombine what it already contains. The compression hierarchy is the mechanism by which novelty is produced. The ecology of feedback loops is the mechanism by which it is threatened. And the distortion measure that determines which configurations count as worth preserving — that remains the task the hierarchy points toward without reaching. It may be the task that remains when all the others have been compressed away.

7 Appendix A: Formal Foundations

This appendix develops the formal constructions that the main text references but does not derive. Each section is self-contained and addresses a specific question a sceptical reader would reasonably ask.

7.1 A.1 Rate-distortion and the compression hierarchy

The compression continuum described in Section 1 rests on rate-distortion theory. This section states the framework precisely and shows how the three compression levels — perceptual, structural, semantic — correspond to rate-distortion problems with different distortion measures.

7.1.1 The rate-distortion function

For a source \(X\) with distribution \(p(x)\), a reproduction alphabet \(\hat{X}\), and a distortion measure \(d: X \times \hat{X} \to [0, \infty)\), the rate-distortion function is

\[ R(D) = \min_{p(\hat{x}|x): \mathbb{E}[d(X, \hat{X})] \leq D} I(X; \hat{X}) \tag{1}\]

where the minimisation is over all conditional distributions (encodings) achieving expected distortion at most \(D\) (Shannon, 1959). \(R(D)\) gives the minimum number of bits required to represent the source at distortion level \(D\).

The key point for the framework: \(R(D)\) is defined relative to the distortion measure \(d\). Change \(d\) and you change everything — which encodings are optimal, what information is preserved, what is discarded. The distortion measure is where relevance enters. It is not a property of the source but a choice made by or on behalf of the receiver.

7.1.2 Three distortion measures, three compression levels

The compression continuum (Section 1.2) identifies three regions. Each corresponds to a class of distortion measure operating at a different level of abstraction.

Perceptual compression. The distortion measure operates on surface features as perceived by a receiver:

\[ d_{\text{percept}}(x, \hat{x}) = \|f_{\text{percept}}(x) - f_{\text{percept}}(\hat{x})\| \tag{2}\]

where \(f_{\text{percept}}\) maps signals to their perceptual representations. JPEG’s quantisation strategy and MP3’s psychoacoustic masking are both solutions to \(R(D)\) under perceptual distortion measures — they discard what the receiver’s sensory system cannot resolve.

Structural compression. The distortion measure operates on relational and compositional structure:

\[ d_{\text{struct}}(x, \hat{x}) = \Delta\bigl(S(x),\, S(\hat{x})\bigr) \tag{3}\]

where \(S(\cdot)\) extracts structural properties (syntactic parse, taxonomic relations, principal components) and \(\Delta\) is a metric on structural descriptions. A grammar that generates invalid sentences incurs high structural distortion regardless of surface similarity. PCA achieves optimal structural compression when the distortion measure is reconstruction error in the subspace of maximum variance.

Semantic compression. The distortion measure operates on meaning — the geometry of inferential relationships:

\[ d_{\text{sem}}(x, \hat{x}) = \Delta\bigl(M(x),\, M(\hat{x})\bigr) \tag{4}\]

where \(M(\cdot)\) maps to a semantic representation in which distance reflects inferential proximity. Two paraphrases incur zero semantic distortion despite maximal surface difference. Two sentences differing by a single negation incur high semantic distortion despite minimal surface difference.

7.1.3 Connection to deep representations

Tishby & Zaslavsky (2015) showed that each layer of a deep network can be understood as solving a rate-distortion problem, with the task label defining relevance. Achille & Soatto (2018) proved a stronger result: the optimal solution to the layer-wise rate-distortion problem — the minimal sufficient statistic for the task — naturally produces representations that are invariant to nuisance factors and disentangled with respect to the task-relevant variables. These properties are not imposed by architectural design. They are consequences of rate-distortion optimality.

The deep variational information bottleneck (Alemi et al., 2017) provides a tractable approximation: rather than solving Equation 1 exactly, each layer minimises a variational upper bound on mutual information with the input while maximising a lower bound on mutual information with the task. Dubois et al. (2020) extend this to the decodable information bottleneck, which ensures that preserved information is not merely present but linearly accessible — a property the main text’s discussion of “making implicit structure explicit” (Section 2.2) requires.

The compression continuum, then, is not a metaphor. It is a claim that different layers solve Equation 1 under distortion measures that shift from surface toward meaning — and that this shift is what the forward pass does.

7.2 A.2 Statistical complexity: definition and properties

Section 2.3 claims that what grows across transformer layers is statistical complexity — a measure of representational organisation that is not constrained by the data processing inequality. This section defines the quantity precisely and explains why the DPI does not bound it.

7.2.1 Causal states and \(\varepsilon\)-machines

Computational mechanics (Crutchfield & Young, 1989; Shalizi & Crutchfield, 2001) defines causal states as equivalence classes over histories that yield identical conditional distributions over futures. For a stationary process \(\ldots X_{-2}, X_{-1}, X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots\), the causal state at time \(t\) is

\[ \epsilon_t = \epsilon(\overleftarrow{x}_t) \quad \text{where} \quad \overleftarrow{x}_t \sim \overleftarrow{x}_t' \iff P(\overrightarrow{X} \mid \overleftarrow{X} = \overleftarrow{x}_t) = P(\overrightarrow{X} \mid \overleftarrow{X} = \overleftarrow{x}_t') \tag{5}\]

where \(\overleftarrow{x}_t\) denotes the past and \(\overrightarrow{X}\) the future. Two histories belong to the same causal state if and only if they make identical predictions.

The \(\varepsilon\)-machine is the hidden Markov model whose states are the causal states and whose transitions are determined by the process. It is the minimal sufficient statistic for prediction — the smallest representation that captures all predictable structure in the process (Shalizi & Crutchfield, 2001).

Statistical complexity is the entropy of the causal state distribution:

\[ C_\mu = H(\epsilon) = -\sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} P(s) \log P(s) \tag{6}\]

This measures how much information is required to specify which causal state the process is in — equivalently, how much memory the minimal predictor must maintain.

7.2.2 Why \(C_\mu\) is not bounded by the DPI

The data processing inequality states that for a Markov chain \(X \to Y \to Z\):

\[ I(X; Z) \leq I(X; Y) \]

This bounds mutual information between the representation and the source. Statistical complexity is not mutual information with the source. It is entropy of the representation’s own causal state structure — a property of how the representation is organised, not how much it remembers about the input.

A concrete example: consider two representations of the same data, both retaining the same mutual information with the source. One stores raw features. The other has reorganised those features into clusters, hierarchies, and compositional structures. They satisfy the DPI equally. But the second has higher statistical complexity — more internal organisation, more structure that a downstream process can exploit.

This is the formal basis for the framework’s central distinction between subtractive processing (discarding information, bounded by DPI) and generative processing (reorganising preserved information, not bounded by DPI).

7.2.3 Relationship to excess entropy

Statistical complexity is related to but distinct from excess entropy (also called predictive information), defined as the mutual information between past and future:

\[ E = I(\overleftarrow{X}; \overrightarrow{X}) \]

Grassberger (1986) showed that \(E \leq C_\mu\) always holds, with equality only for processes where the causal states are deterministically related to the past. Excess entropy measures how much of the past is relevant to the future; statistical complexity measures how much memory is needed to use that relevance. The gap between them reflects the cost of organising predictive information into a usable form — precisely the cost the framework claims transformer layers pay.

7.2.4 Estimation challenges

Direct estimation of \(C_\mu\) in high-dimensional continuous spaces — such as transformer hidden states — is technically formidable. The causal state construction requires partitioning history space into equivalence classes, which in continuous settings demands either discretisation (which introduces artefacts) or kernel-based estimation (which scales poorly with dimension).

This is why the main text (Section 2.3) relies on proxy measures:

  • Intrinsic dimensionality estimates the effective number of degrees of freedom in the representation manifold (Ansuini et al., 2019). Growth in intrinsic dimensionality is necessary but not sufficient for growth in \(C_\mu\) — a representation can have high dimensionality without high organisation.
  • Participation ratio measures how many dimensions carry significant variance. Like intrinsic dimensionality, it tracks geometric complexity without directly measuring causal state structure.

The framework’s predictions are stated in terms of these proxies where direct \(C_\mu\) estimation is not feasible, with explicit acknowledgment that the proxy relationship is imperfect. A method for estimating statistical complexity in transformer hidden states would be a significant contribution — and would make the framework’s predictions directly testable rather than testable by proxy.

7.3 A.3 The autoregressive loop as sequential coding

Section 3.2 frames the autoregressive loop as a sequential coding scheme. This section states the bandwidth mismatch formally and explains why classical channel coding theorems apply only approximately.

7.3.1 The dimensionality mismatch

At each generation step, the model’s internal state is a vector \(\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^d\) where \(d\) is the model dimension (typically \(10^3\) to \(10^4\)). The output is a single token \(t \in \mathcal{V}\) where \(|\mathcal{V}|\) is the vocabulary size (typically \(3 \times 10^4\) to \(10^5\)).

The information content of the internal state — even after accounting for the fact that hidden states occupy a lower-dimensional manifold — far exceeds what a single token can carry. If the effective dimensionality of the hidden state manifold is \(d_{\text{eff}}\) and each dimension carries \(b\) bits of useful information, the internal information is \(O(d_{\text{eff}} \cdot b)\) bits, while a single token carries at most \(\log_2 |\mathcal{V}|\) bits (approximately 15–17 bits for typical vocabularies).

This is a bandwidth mismatch in the precise Shannon sense. The source rate exceeds the channel capacity per use.

7.3.2 Sequential coding as the solution

Shannon’s channel coding theorem (Shannon, 1948) establishes that reliable communication at rates exceeding channel capacity per use requires multiple channel uses — distributing the message across a sequence of transmissions. The autoregressive loop implements exactly this: each token is a channel use, and the sequence of tokens collectively encodes what no single token could carry.

The classical theorem assumes a fixed message encoded before transmission begins. The autoregressive setting departs from this in a critical way: the “message” is constructed during transmission. Each token both extends the message and encodes accumulated content for subsequent decoding. This is closer to joint source-channel coding, where source coding (deciding what to say) and channel coding (deciding how to encode it for the channel) happen simultaneously.

7.3.3 Successive refinement

The connection to successive refinement (Equitz & Cover, 1991) is direct. A source is successively refinable if it can be encoded in stages, with each stage adding detail, such that the overall encoding is as efficient as encoding at the final resolution in one pass. Gaussian sources are successively refinable; many other sources are not.

The autoregressive loop performs a form of successive refinement — each token adds to the encoded message — but with the complication that the “source” (the intended output) is not fully determined when encoding begins. The model is simultaneously deciding what to say and encoding it, with each token constraining both the message and its encoding.

This departure from the classical setting means that standard successive refinement guarantees do not directly apply. The effective capacity per token is not \(\log_2 |\mathcal{V}|\) but something lower, reduced by the overhead of message construction. How much lower is an empirical question the framework highlights but does not answer.

7.3.4 Self-attention as a capacity constraint

Hahn (2020) proved formal limitations on the computational capacity of self-attention: hard attention transformers cannot model all regular languages, and the class of functions computable by self-attention is constrained by the interaction between sequence length and embedding dimension. These limitations are consistent with the bandwidth framing. Self-attention is the mechanism by which the model reads the accumulated context — the sequence of previous channel uses — and its computational constraints bound how effectively the model can decode the information distributed across those uses.

The practical consequence: even when the context window is technically large enough to contain all necessary information, attentional capacity limits how much of that distributed encoding the model can effectively integrate. This is the formal basis for the attentional degradation discussed in Section 3.7 — a hard constraint distinct from the compositional degradation that arises from regime drift.

7.4 A.4 The Saxe/Shwartz-Ziv measurement debate

The framework’s predictions about information flow across layers (Section 2.2, Section 2.4) inherit a known measurement problem. This section summarises the debate fairly and explains why the framework’s predictions survive it.

7.4.1 The original claim and the challenge

Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby (2017) reported that deep networks exhibit two distinct training phases: an initial fitting phase where mutual information \(I(X; T)\) between input and hidden layers increases, followed by a compression phase where \(I(X; T)\) decreases while task-relevant information \(I(T; Y)\) is preserved. This was presented as evidence for the information bottleneck theory of deep learning.

Saxe et al. (2018) challenged this finding on two grounds. First, the compression phase was observed with saturating nonlinearities (tanh) but not with ReLU activations — suggesting the finding was activation-function-dependent rather than a general property of deep learning. Second, they argued that the mutual information estimates were sensitive to binning choices, and that for deterministic networks (networks with deterministic activation functions), \(I(X; T)\) is technically infinite unless noise is added or binning is applied.

7.4.2 The measurement problem

The core issue is that mutual information between continuous random variables is sensitive to how continuity is handled. For a deterministic function \(f\), the mutual information \(I(X; f(X))\) is infinite — the mapping preserves all information. The finite values reported by Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby (2017) depended on binning (discretising) the hidden layer activations, and the results were sensitive to bin width.

Subsequent work has attempted to resolve this in several ways:

  • Goldfeld et al. (2019) developed neural network-based estimators that avoid binning, finding evidence of compression that is less sensitive to architectural choices.
  • Chelombiev et al. (2019) used adaptive estimators and found compression in both tanh and ReLU networks, suggesting the original null result for ReLU was an estimation artefact.
  • Kolchinsky & Tracey (2018) showed that in deterministic networks, a noise-based definition of mutual information (adding small noise to activations) recovers meaningful compression results.
  • Wickstrøm et al. (2022) used matrix-based Rényi entropy, which avoids binning entirely, and found compression across architectures.
  • Gabrié et al. (2018) analysed mutual information in analytically tractable network models, providing ground truth against which estimators can be calibrated.

Geiger (2021) provides a comprehensive review. The emerging consensus is that some form of information compression occurs across layers, but its magnitude, timing, and relationship to generalisation depend on the estimation method, architecture, and training regime. Amjad & Geiger (2020) and Goldfeld & Polyanskiy (2020) offer further perspectives on when and how the information bottleneck principle applies in practice.

7.4.3 Why the framework’s predictions survive this debate

The framework does not depend on the strong version of the Shwartz-Ziv claim — that a distinct compression phase is a universal feature of deep learning. It depends on two weaker claims, both of which survive the measurement debate:

  1. Reorganisation occurs. Even when total mutual information with the input does not measurably decrease, the structure of the representation changes across layers. This is observable through probing studies, representational similarity analysis, and intrinsic dimensionality measurements — none of which depend on mutual information estimation. The debate is about whether reorganisation is accompanied by measurable compression. The framework requires only that reorganisation occurs, not that it is always accompanied by a detectable drop in \(I(X; T)\).

  2. The DPI permits structural growth. Whether or not \(I(X; T)\) decreases, the DPI guarantees it does not increase. The framework’s claim is that statistical complexity — representational organisation — grows within this constraint. This claim is orthogonal to the measurement debate, which concerns the subtractive side (how much information is discarded) rather than the generative side (how remaining information is organised).

The measurement debate does, however, constrain how the framework’s predictions should be tested. Predictions involving mutual information (Prediction 4) should be tested with estimation methods robust to the issues identified above — ideally multiple methods, to ensure results are not artefacts of a particular estimator. Predictions involving statistical complexity (Prediction 1, Prediction 2) face their own estimation challenges (Section 7.2) but are not affected by the mutual information measurement debate, since they concern a different quantity entirely.
